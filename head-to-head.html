The Head-to-Head (v0.2)

All four options now pressure-tested — updated verdicts, the corrected Option 3 framing, Option 4 downgraded, and the recommendation rewritten.

11 min readClassification · Strategic / InternalSource · ftlab-org-architecture-head-to-head-v0.2.md
In brief

The v0.2 comparison, folding in independent red-teams of all four options. The spine of the recommendation survives, but the red-teams sharpen it: Option 3’s “deployable now” is partly a sleight of hand, Option 4 drops from north-star to a source of four steal-ables, and one deferred fix — independent Guardianship at 2–5 humans — is promoted to the top priority.

Key takeaways
  • All four options are now adversarially reviewed to the same standard (verdicts in §2/§4).
  • The #1 cross-cutting unsolved Critical: independent Guardianship at 2–5 humans — solve it now (externalise the halt-right), don’t defer it.
  • Option 3 reframed honestly: ship Option 1 now; build and red-team the Core on a timeboxed track; migrate per-task-class — not “instant.”
  • Option 4 downgraded to a far-future hypothesis + the four steal-ables; its distinctive mechanism can’t work at FTLAB’s scale.
  • Factual correction: Alloy/OneHealth has launched zero companies — re-grade the “proven in production” claim.

This revision folds in independent adversarial red-teams of all four options — not just Option 1. Each option was reviewed to the same standard (severity-rated findings, concrete fixes, a verdict). The comparison, the gaps, and the recommendation are updated accordingly. The headline change from v0.1: the red-teams materially shift the conclusion — Option 3's "deployable now" is partly a sleight of hand, Option 4 drops from "north-star" to "a source of four steal-ables," and one fix that all four options defer — independent Guardianship at 2–5 humans — is promoted to the single most important thing to solve.

0. What changed since v0.1

  • All four options now have a standalone red-team (ftlab-pressure-test-option-2/3/4-v0.1.md, plus the original ftlab-org-architecture-pressure-test-v0.1.md for Option 1).
  • Verdicts updated (§2, §4).
  • Option 3 reframed honestly — the core diagnosis survives, but "one build / deployable now / closes the Critical finding" does not (§4, §7).
  • Option 4 downgraded — from "the destination/north-star" to "a source of four steal-ables + a far-future hypothesis" (§4, §7).
  • A new cross-cutting section (§5) — the patterns that hold across all four, led by the recurring unsolved Critical: independent Guardianship at small scale.
  • A factual correction (§6) that affects the evidence base for several documents.
  • The recommendation rewritten (§8) around what survives all four critiques.

1. The four options in a sentence each (recap)

  • Option 1 — Seven Domains. Legible department model: seven AI Domain-heads under a thin human Cortex. Deployable now. Answers: who is accountable?
  • Option 2 — Core · Engines · Edge. Coordination as a built substrate (Core), Engines do the work, a flat human Edge holds accountability. Answers: how does coordination happen?
  • Option 3 — The Integrated. Option 1's legible face on Option 2's engineered Core, reached by a phased sequence. Positioned in v0.1 as the recommendation.
  • Option 4 — The New Mode. A constitutional core over a price-and-pressure market/ecology of venture-cells. Positioned in v0.1 as the destination.

3. The four advocacy positions, now tempered

The advocacy briefs still stand as the strongest case for each — but the red-teams puncture three of their sharpest claims:

  • Option 2's advocate claimed "Option 2's problems are bugs (unfinished carpentry); Option 1's are the design." The red-team rebuts this: at least four of Option 2's hardest problems — the singleton Core, the unmeetable artifact-substrate precondition, unstaffable separation-of-powers, and a renamed central coordinator — are architectural, not unfinished work. The asymmetry the advocate leaned on is false.
  • Option 3's advocate claimed the combination is "one build, not two" and "deployable now" while "closing the Critical finding." The red-team shows these are true at different times presented as simultaneous (§4).
  • Option 4's advocate positioned it as the destination the trajectory walks toward. The red-team finds its distinctive mechanism (venture-cell selection) can't work at any scale the framework permits — so it is not a reachable destination, but a source of portable ideas.

4. The four red-teams in brief

Option 1 — Seven Domains · salvageable with significant changes. Memory-as-coordinator was asserted, not engineered (fixed by elevating D2 to a real substrate); Guardian independence unsolved at 2–5 people; the integrator carries the keystone risk. (Already folded into v0.3.)

Option 2 — Core · Engines · Edge · salvageable with significant changes (3 Critical, 7 Major).

  • Critical — The Core is a singleton; the boundary layer (C2a) relocates the failure rather than removing it (C2a is itself a hand-curated table with the same staleness disease, has no row for novel cross-Engine cases, and there is no rollback once a bad world-model write has propagated to every Engine).
  • Critical — "Nothing of consequence happens off-substrate" is a binary the design can only meet partially, and the blind spots correlate with the highest-stakes (relational/sovereign) work — the model is confidently blind exactly where being wrong is most expensive.
  • Critical — Separation-of-powers is near-impossible at 2–5 humans, and fractal instantiation multiplies the problem.
  • Plus: topology-by-task is an un-owned high-variance singleton judgment; "verification scales with throughput" is half-true (fails at the high-stakes head); fractal multiplies instances but doesn't coordinate them; it does not avoid the central coordinator, it centralises it and renames it "the Core."

Option 3 — The Integrated · salvageable with significant changes.

  • Critical — "One build / deployable now / closes the Critical finding" is a sleight of hand: during the entire Core build the legible face runs on Option 1's coordination — the exact mechanism Option 1's red-team rated Critical. The claims are true at different times, presented as one.
  • During the interim it is nearer the union of the two options' flaws than their strengths; the shared Core has had no adversarial review of its own; the canonical mapping table is built to drift (a fixed accountability ontology mapped to a deliberately-moving Core, with no owner or audit); Phase 1 is mid-flight re-platforming with two live coordination systems; and framing it as "the recommendation" lets the founders default into the most complex path without deciding which primitive leads.

Option 4 — The New Mode · flawed at foundation as a destination.

  • Critical — Venture-cell "selection" can't work at any scale the framework permits — a statistical-power problem (≈5 expensive ventures, multi-year feedback, an un-attributable fitness signal, an explicit no-proliferation discipline), not a cold-start. The genome accumulates superstition, not capability.
  • Critical — It doesn't escape the central coordinator; it relocates control to the fitness function, prices, constitution, and genome — central planning by the same 2–5 people, more concentrated and less legible than a named coordinator.
  • Critical — A curated, homogeneous genome amplifies and stabilises LLM-agent collusion (best-evidenced finding); verification cost plausibly exceeds the coordination saving for FTLAB's tightly-coupled regulated work.
  • Critical — Rules-as-code can't carry open-textured Values (Stewardship, Coherence, the Widening of We) — they compile to gameable proxies or smuggle a human interpreter back in.
  • What survives: the four steal-ables and the correct question ("what is FTLAB's institutional form?").

5. Cross-cutting findings (true across all four)

  1. Independent Guardianship at 2–5 humans is the recurring unsolved Critical. It appears in 1, 2, 3, and 4; every option defers it ("as the team grows"). It is also the one a regulator/sovereign will not let you defer (a director must be a natural person; a values-judge who carries P&L is not independent; an agent guardian inherits the same humans' values). This is now the single most important thing to solve — and it is solvable today (see §8).
  2. "Central coordinator by another name." Options 2 and 4 both claim to dissolve central coordination and then rebuild it — Option 2 in the Core, Option 4 in the fitness-function + constitution + genome. The honest framing is engineering quality of the centre, not absence of one. (Option 1 is honest that it has a centre; that candour is a point in its favour.)
  3. Different kinds of problems. Option 2's hardest problems are architectural; Option 3's is honesty about the interim; Option 4's are foundational. Carpentry can be finished; architecture and foundations cannot be refactored away.
  4. Concentration beats illegibility. Across all four, the reviews favour a named, legible centre with an external check over a diffuse or renamed one — because the failure mode of "no legible authority" is that informal/agent-default authority fills the vacuum unaccountably.

6. A factual correction to the evidence base

Several documents cited Alloy / OneHealth — an agent-native venture studio — as proof the agent-orchestration pattern "works in production" (1,133 agents across "9 companies"). The Option 2 red-team verified this: Alloy has launched zero companies; the 1,133 agents are internal infrastructure only. Re-grade every "proven in production" claim that leans on Alloy to aspirational, and find a second precedent before treating the pattern as validated. (This does not sink any option — but it removes a load-bearing piece of evidence the optimistic readings used.)

7. Gaps and recommended fixes (updated, per option)

Option 1 — adopt the boundary layer into D2; add topology-by-task to charters; make the artifact substrate an org-wide day-zero discipline; set a dated Guardian-externalisation trigger (not "as the team grows").

Option 2 — engineer C2a to the world-model's own standard and fail safe (unknown decision-classes default to human-decided); replace the substrate binary with a per-class coverage map (low-coverage outputs marked low-confidence by construction); add blast-radius control + a coordination circuit-breaker + rollback; make Core-first a dependency rule, not a calendar gate (run a thin vertical slice in parallel); name the legal persona and externalise the Guardian now; red-team the Core before anything relies on it.

Option 3 — split the timeline honestly (deployable-now vs Core-built-later are different milestones); budget and timebox the Core and red-team it as a Phase-0 gate; engineer the mapping table as coordination-critical state with an owner + a quarterly represented-vs-real audit; migrate per-task-class with shadow-mode comparison; triage the six concurrent workstreams into Now/Next/Watch; and force the real decision — which primitive leads — rather than defaulting into the maximal path.

Option 4 — split the two markets (keep an internal agent-task market where density is reachable; retire venture-cell "selection" as the framing); scope rules-as-code to bright-line guards with a named human values-authority; demote reputation from allocator to input to a human autonomy decision (with an exploration floor + concentration cap); sever the external protocol from the internal spine and default to conventional auditable infrastructure (gate on a DFSA read); and treat the whole thing as a far-future hypothesis to falsify, not a near-term build.

8. The recommendation (v0.2)

The earlier recommendation's spine survives, but the red-teams sharpen it and add one priority the v0.1 version under-weighted.

The robust path, in order:

  1. Deploy Option 1 now — with its own fixes baked in (the boundary layer, topology-by-task, the artifact-substrate discipline). It is the only deployable, legible, reversible, value-producing structure, and it is honest that it has a centre.
  2. Solve the Guardian / separation-of-powers problem now, not "as the team grows." This is the #1 cross-cutting unsolved Critical. Externalise the halt-right to a board member or an independent reviewer with a real, exercisable veto; name the legal persona; make the "never"-rules discretion-free so they bind a conflicted founder. Until then, log it as Alignment Debt and treat it as the top open risk — but do not pretend it is handled.
  3. Build the Core on a timeboxed, adversarially-reviewed track — and be honest it is not instant. Re-state Option 3 truthfully: deploy Option 1 now; build and red-team the Core in parallel; migrate per-task-class with shadow-mode once the Core is verified. Do not claim "deployable now" and "closes the Critical finding" in the same breath — they happen at different times, and the interim runs on coordination that itself needs the boundary-layer fix to be safe.
  4. Adopt the four steal-ables inside the skeleton — price human attention (a budget to escalate), reputation as an input to autonomy (not an allocator), Values compiled to bright-line rules-as-code, and a pressure/selection signal on the substrate. These improve whatever you build and are the durable residue of Option 4.
  5. Treat Option 4 as a far-future hypothesis, not a destination. Its distinctive mechanism can't work at FTLAB's scale; re-evaluate only if the agent population and venture portfolio ever reach the density a market needs. Don't aim the architecture at it today.
  6. Prove every step on one venture before the portfolio, with instrumented decision-quality and a pre-committed rollback.

The decision that must not be dodged: Option 3 as written lets the founders say "yes" to everything and default into the most complex path. The real fork is which primitive leads — deploy-the-department-and-harden-it (Option 1-led) versus build-the-Core-and-let-the-org-fall-out (Option 2-led). The honest recommendation is Option 1-led with the Core built underneath on a separate, red-teamed track — because that is the only sequence where something legible and accountable is running the whole time, and the riskier component (the Core) must prove itself before anything depends on it.

9. The genuine founder decisions (updated)

  1. The Guardian — now the top decision. Externalise the halt-right to a board/independent reviewer now (recommended), or run a logged, discretion-free conflict-of-interest protocol with a dated trigger? No option works without answering this.
  2. Which primitive leads — Option 1-led (department-first, harden it, build the Core underneath) vs Option 2-led (Core-first)? (Recommendation: Option 1-led.)
  3. Timebox the Core build — what is the deadline and the Phase-0 red-team gate before any reliance on it?
  4. Adopt the four steal-ables now? (Recommended — priced attention and reputation-as-input are design-shaping.)
  5. Re-grade the Alloy precedent and decide whether a second real-world proof point is needed before scaling the agent-orchestration pattern.

10. Status

All four options are documented and now all four are adversarially reviewed. This v0.2 supersedes the v0.1 head-to-head. Nothing is deployed; everything is built, saved, and ready for the founders' decision — with the Guardian/separation-of-powers question elevated to the top.
