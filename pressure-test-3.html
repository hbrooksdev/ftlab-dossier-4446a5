Pressure-Test — Option 3

Independent adversarial review of the Integrated Architecture — where the “deployable now” claim breaks.

Option 343 min readClassification · Strategic / InternalSource · ftlab-pressure-test-option-3-v0.1.md
In brief

A standalone red-team of Option 3. Verdict: salvageable with significant changes — the diagnosis (one structure at two altitudes) survives, but the “one build, deployable now, closes the Critical finding” claim does not.

Key takeaways
  • Critical: “one build / deployable now” is a sleight of hand — during the whole Core build the legible face runs on the exact coordination Option 1’s red-team rated Critical.
  • During the interim it is nearer the union of the two options’ flaws than their strengths; the shared Core has had no adversarial review of its own.
  • The canonical mapping table is built to drift — a fixed accountability ontology mapped to a deliberately-moving Core, with no owner or audit.
  • Phase 1 is mid-flight re-platforming with two live coordination systems — the riskiest step, and the least specified.
  • “The recommendation” lets the founders default into the most complex path without deciding which primitive leads.

This is an adversarial review commissioned in the same spirit as the Option 1 pressure-test: the job is to find where Option 3 breaks, say why it matters, rate severity, and recommend a concrete fix — not to cheerlead. It steelmans each move before attacking it. It does not relitigate the AI-Born thesis (conceded as direction) or re-derive Options 1, 2, and 4 (reviewed elsewhere). It attacks the integration itself: its central "one build, not two" claim, its inheritance of both parents' flaws, its two-vocabulary reconciliation, its mid-flight re-platforming, its scope, its permanent dual-maintenance, its unsolved separation-of-powers, and its function as "the recommendation." Where Option 3 is genuinely robust, that is recorded in "What survives the critique." Evidence is graded inline. The verdict is at the end.

0. Summary of the design under review

Option 3 proposes that Options 1 and 2 are "one design seen at two altitudes" and specifies running them as a single system: Option 1's named Domains + human Cortex as the human-facing accountability face, on top of Option 2's engineered Core as the coordination engine, reached by a four-phase sequence (Phase 0 foundations → Phase 1 coordination proven → Phase 2 migrate Domains→Engines on one venture → Phase 3 fractal scale-out + measured devolution), with four "steal-able" upgrades from Option 4 wired in at Phase 1, aiming at the Option 4 market/genome "destination." The end-state is a permanent hybrid: keep both faces forever, reconciled by a single canonical mapping table. The one permanent structural change is that D2 is "demoted from the Domain that coordinates to the steward of the Core." Its central claim (§9): Option 3 "ships Option 1's legible, reversible, value-producing org now while building Option 2's engineered Core underneath, closing the one Critical finding and paying neither's full penalty."

The design is intelligent, unusually self-aware about its own risks (§7 is a genuine risk table, not decoration), and largely faithful to the framework's vocabulary. The findings below are where it nonetheless breaks, overclaims, or defers the real decision.

A note on evidence before the findings. I verified the artifact claims on disk. Option 3 §5 says it reuses "the five staged Engine definitions + the routing and verification agents (rollout-option2/agents/)" and Option 1's "seven staged Head definitions (rollout/agents/)." Both directories exist and each contains seven staged agent definition files plus a ROLLOUT-PLAN. That claim is accurate and is graded strong. But the same check exposes the central problem (Finding 1): those files are agent charters and build specifications, not a running system, and the build-spec itself (…build-spec-v0.1.md §0) classes the Core (C1–C3) as "Infrastructure (built, not an agent)" that does not yet exist, while rollout-option2/ROLLOUT-PLAN.md (lines 11, 33–52, 75–77) states the Core is a multi-phase build that must be completed in a fixed order before any Engine is deployed — "Build it in that order or do not build it." The disk confirms the parts that are specified; it also confirms the part that is unbuilt is the part Option 3 leans on.

1. "One build, not two" is a sleight of hand — the legible face runs on the unbuilt Core's absence, on human-curated coordination, for the entire Option-2 programme

The claim. Option 3 §3 and §7, and the advocacy brief §3, assert that deploying Option 1 now and "building D2 to the Core spec from day zero" is one effort, not two, because "Option 1's Step 1 is 'build D2's memory substrate first'; Option 2's Phase 0 is 'build C1 → C2 first' — these are the same first move." The risk table (§7) restates it: "Most of it is one build, not two." The headline (§9): deployable "now."

Steelman. There is a real and non-trivial truth here. Both rollouts genuinely do name "build the coordination substrate first" as step one, and it would be wasteful to build a throwaway D2 memory and then a separate Core. Building D2 to the Core spec from day zero avoids one rebuild, and the instinct to spend the substrate budget once, on the better specification, is correct. To that extent the integration is cheaper than doing the two sequentially.

Where it breaks. "One build, not two" conflates the substrate build with the whole architecture, and "deployable now" conflates the legible face with the system the case sells.

  1. The honest decomposition is: a small deployable face + the entire long, unproven Option-2 Core programme. Option 1 is deployable now because its coordination is a human-curated memory plus an integrator chartered to route-and-surface (Option 1 §2, §5; charters doc §0). Option 2's Core is a four-to-five-phase engineering programme — artifact substrate → boundary layer → world-model-as-a-database (freshness, role/confidence conflict-merge, provenance, out-of-band verification) → capability registry → verification layer → routing agent — that its own rollout plan says must be built in that order, in full, before any Engine deploys (rollout-option2/ROLLOUT-PLAN.md §3, §6). Option 3 does not shorten that programme by a single step. It inherits all of it. So the true sum is Option 1's deployable skeleton plus 100% of Option 2's Core build — which is more total work than either option alone, not "one build." The "same first move" is one shared step, not one shared programme; the rhetorical move quietly promotes the shared step to the whole.
  1. "Deployable now" is true only of the face, and the face's coordination is the rejected mechanism. Option 3's own Phase 0 exit trigger is explicit and damning: "the Core passes Option 2's verification checklist on the studio itself. Until then, D2 stays human-curated and the org runs on Option 1's charters as written" (§3, Phase 0; restated in §7's row 2 and the brief §3). Read plainly: for the entire duration of the Core build — months, by Option 2's own framing of a "long pre-value Core build" (head-to-head matrix; case-for-option-2 §"weaknesses") — FTLAB runs on exactly the coordination the Option 1 pressure-test rated its single Critical flaw: "memory-as-coordinator is asserted, not engineered" (Option 1 red-team Finding 2). Option 3 markets itself as the design that closes that Critical finding "on day one" (§3, Phase 0: "This closes the Critical finding on day one"). It does not. It closes it on the day the Core passes verification — an unbounded future date gated on the hardest, least-proven build in the whole programme. On day one, and for the whole of Phase 0, the Critical finding is still open and is what the institution runs on. The claim "closes the one Critical finding" and the claim "deployable now" are true at different times and the document presents them as simultaneous.
  1. The gating is honest but it converts "deployable now" into "legible-but-uncoordinated-by-engineering now." The hard gate (§7 row 2: "until the Core passes verification, D2 stays human-curated") is the right safety behaviour — but it is the admission that the engineered coordination is not available at deployment. What is deployable now is a named org chart whose coordination substrate is the asserted-not-engineered one. That is a perfectly defensible interim, but it is Option 1's interim, with Option 1's Critical risk live, not a new thing Option 3 has solved.

Why it matters. The single most persuasive sentence in the whole Option 3 case — "one build, not two… deployable now while closing the Critical finding" — is the load-bearing claim that makes Option 3 look strictly dominant over both parents. It is a composition of two separately-true half-claims that are false in conjunction. A founder reading §9 will believe they are buying Option 1's readiness and the closure of its Critical flaw at the same time, for the price of one build. They are buying Option 1's readiness now, the Option-2 build in full, and the closure of the Critical flaw only when that build succeeds — with the institution exposed to the Critical flaw in the interim. This is the same class of move the Option 1 red-team flagged as hiding the management function "inside the words 'escalate' and 'shared memory'" (Finding 1) — here the cost and the timeline are hidden inside the words "one build" and "now."

Severity: Critical. (It is the central justification for preferring Option 3, and it does not hold as stated.)

Fix.

  1. Re-state the claim honestly and split the timeline. Say: "Option 3 deploys Option 1's legible face now, running on Option 1's human-curated coordination — which carries Option 1's Critical keystone risk in the interim — while building Option 2's Core as a separate, multi-phase programme underneath; the Critical finding is closed only when the Core passes verification, not at deployment." Drop "one build, not two" or demote it to "one shared first step; the Core build is otherwise inherited in full."
  2. Put a dated, resourced budget on the Core programme, and an explicit interim-risk register for the Phase-0 window: what coordination failures Option 1's human-curated memory is exposed to (memory poisoning, stale state, contested-routing escalation load) while the Core is being built, and who watches them. The gate protects against trusting a half-built Core; it does nothing for the risk of running the un-engineered coordination for months.
  3. Define a Phase-0 abort/timebox. If the Core has not passed verification by a named date or budget, the founders face a real decision (stay on Option 1 permanently; cut scope; re-spec). Without this, "build the Core underneath" is an open-ended commitment dressed as a free inheritance.

Evidence grade: Strong. The contradicting text is in Option 3's own §3 and §7 and in Option 2's own rollout plan; the artifact-existence check confirms specs exist but the Core does not.

2. Option 3 is closer to the union of both parents' flaws than to the union of their strengths

The claim. Option 3 §7 closes: "The integration adds no new fatal risk; it removes Option 1's Critical keystone risk and Option 2's readiness/legibility risk." The brief §6 repeats it verbatim. The framing throughout is that each parent's fatal weakness is the other's core strength, so combining cancels both.

Steelman. The "fatal weakness is the other's strength" pairing is partly real and is the best idea in the integration. Option 2's engineered Core genuinely is the specification Option 1's keystone lacked (Option 1 red-team Finding 2 fix: "Specify D2 as a coordination system… freshness SLA, conflict-resolution protocol, provenance model" — which is line-for-line Option 2's C2). And Option 1's named seats genuinely are the legible accountability surface Option 2 lacked (case-for-option-2 §"weaknesses": "accountability specified as functions not named people"). Where the two flaws are complementary, integration does cancel them.

Where it breaks. "No new fatal risk" is asserted, not argued, and it is false on inspection — and several inherited flaws are not cancelled by the other parent because they are not the kind of flaw the other parent addresses.

  • Inherited from Option 1, not cancelled by Option 2: the "rebuilds the routing hierarchy / AI-enabled cut" critique. Option 2's own advocacy (case-for-option-2; head-to-head Position 2) says Option 1's "biggest weakness is its chosen primitive (a department chart of agent-managers) — not fixable without becoming Option 2." Option 3 keeps the seven named Domains permanently as the accountability face (§2: "keep them, permanently") and only migrates them to Engines at Phase 2, "on one venture first," with no committed date for the studio itself. So for the whole of Phases 0–1 — and, for the studio, potentially forever — FTLAB runs the department cut that Option 2 calls the unfixable wrong primitive. Option 3 has not resolved the Option-1-vs-Option-2 primitive dispute; it has deferred it and kept the Option 1 answer as the default. The Core underneath does not cancel this, because the critique is about the named accountability ontology, which Option 3 fixes in place by design.
  • Inherited from Option 2, not cancelled by Option 1: the keystone Core single-point-of-failure. The head-to-head matrix lists Option 2's biggest weakness as "the Core is a single point of failure," and the head-to-head's own fix list says "Red-team the Core the way D2 was." Option 3 moves all coordination into the Core (§2: "everything runs through the Core… Verification is a first-class Core layer") and demotes D2 to "the steward of the Core." This re-concentrates the single-point-of-failure that Option 1's "keystone is one of seven Domains" structure had bounded (head-to-head matrix, "Conceptual risk": Option 1 "Bounded — keystone is one of seven Domains"; Option 2 "Concentrated at the centre"). The Option 1 red-team's Finding 2 sub-point — "The model makes coordination depend entirely on one [thing]… if it drifts, every other Domain silently mis-coordinates. There is no second coordination path" — applies more forcefully to Option 3's Core than to Option 1's D2, because Option 3 has explicitly removed the Domain-boundary firewalling ("no Domain routes information to another Domain; they read/write shared state"). Option 1 at least kept Domains as semi-independent department silos; Option 3 dissolves them into one shared substrate. The Core has never been adversarially hardened (head-to-head: Option 2 "Pressure-tested? No"), and Option 3 inherits an unpressure-tested keystone and makes it more central.
  • The "no new fatal risk" claim ignores integration-specific risks that exist in neither parent: the two-vocabulary drift (Finding 3), the mid-flight re-platforming with two live coordination systems (Finding 4), and the dual-maintenance of the permanent hybrid (Finding 6). These are created by integrating and are addressed below. So the claim is false twice over: it under-counts inherited flaws and ignores born-of-integration flaws.

Why it matters. The entire appeal of Option 3 rests on "captures both strengths, pays neither penalty." The honest accounting is: it captures both strengths eventually and conditionally, pays a reduced version of Option 1's penalty (the Core, once built, fixes the keystone) but a worse version of Option 2's central-fragility penalty (more coordination concentrated in the Core, still unhardened), retains Option 1's primitive critique for the whole interim and possibly forever at the studio, and adds three integration-specific costs. A pessimist's one-line summary, which the document should be made to answer rather than wave away: Option 3 is Option 1's primitive plus Option 2's single-point-of-failure plus the cost of running both — until and unless a long, unhardened build succeeds.

Severity: Major.

Fix.

  1. Replace "adds no new fatal risk" with an explicit inherited-and-new risk ledger. For each of the two parents' top flaws, state whether integration cancels, reduces, retains, or worsens it, with the reasoning. (My reading: Option 1 keystone → reduced-but-only-after-build; Option 1 primitive critique → retained through interim; Option 2 central fragility → worsened; plus three new integration risks.) Intellectual honesty here is cheap and is exactly the posture the framework asks for (conviction without rigidity).
  2. Red-team the Core before betting the integration on it, as the head-to-head itself recommends. Option 3 cannot claim to "close the Critical finding" by re-seating the org on a substrate that has had no adversarial review. Commission the Core pressure-test as a Phase-0 gate, not a later nicety.
  3. Restore a coordination fallback. Because Option 3 dissolves the Domain firewalls that bounded Option 1's blast radius, specify a degraded-mode coordination path (e.g., Domains revert to human-curated hand-offs on a Core integrity-alarm) so a Core drift does not silently mis-coordinate everything at once.

Evidence grade: Strong for the inherited-flaw analysis (drawn from the parents' own docs and the existing red-team); theoretical for the "worsened central fragility" claim (it is an architectural inference, not yet observed — but it follows directly from removing the Domain boundaries).

3. The canonical mapping table is the linchpin — and a fixed accountability ontology bolted to an evolving engineering substrate is built to drift into a represented-vs-real governance gap

The claim. Option 3 §7 (row 1), §6, and the brief §4/§6 make the canonical mapping table (Domains↔Engines, Cortex↔Edge, D2↔Core) "the single source of truth," with the rule "Domains/Cortex = accountability names; Core/Engines/Edge = implementation" and "every charter row points at its boundary-layer row." This is offered as the mechanism that makes the two vocabularies a genuine reconciliation rather than a confusion, and that keeps the legible face "true, not cosmetic" (brief §4).

Steelman. Separating a stable accountability ontology (what a regulator reads) from a moving implementation (how coordination actually runs) is a sound and even elegant idea — it is the right thing to hold fixed (legibility) versus let move (engineering), and Option 3 says so explicitly (§6 Evolution: "the named Domains stay fixed… while the Core's internals and Engine boundaries evolve underneath"). A single mapping table is the correct artifact to carry that correspondence, and pointing each charter row at its boundary-layer row is genuinely good practice.

Where it breaks. The design names the divergence risk and then assumes it away by fiat. The assumptions section concedes the whole thing turns on this: "running a legible face over an efficient substrate does not create a gap between represented and real governance (medium-high if the mapping table is the single source of truth)" (§6). That parenthetical is doing enormous load-bearing work, and three forces push the two sides of the table apart over exactly the timescale the design runs on:

  1. The two sides are governed by different update rules and different cadences. The named ontology is "fixed" by design (§6: "the named Domains stay fixed"). The Core's module boundaries are "adjustable as the capability boundaries are refined" (Option 2 §1, §2.2) and the Engine cut is "one valid cut; adjustable" (Option 2 §2.2). A fixed thing mapped to a deliberately-moving thing does not stay in correspondence unless something actively re-maps it on every Core change. Option 3 names that something ("maintain the canonical mapping table from day one," §8) but assigns it no owner, no update protocol, no review cadence, and no integrity check. This is precisely the failure the Option 1 red-team identified for the memory itself (Finding 2: "Coordination-by-memory fails the instant the memory lags reality… no write-discipline, no freshness SLA"). The mapping table is a second institutional-memory artifact with the same unspecified-maintenance flaw — and unlike the corpus terms, it must be re-verified every time the engineering moves, which is continuously.
  1. The mapping is not "near-exact" at the rows that matter, so the table encodes ambiguity, not truth. Option 3's §1 table is honest enough to label two rows imperfect: D6 ↔ E3+E5 (one Domain to two Engines), D5 ↔ "C5 + the Edge's human Verification/Governance" (one Domain split across an automated layer and two human functions), and D7 ↔ "the Edge (capital/attention, apprenticeship) + Core-instrumented finance" — flagged "partial." A "single source of truth" whose key rows are one-to-two and two-to-one mappings is not a translation table; it is a standing reconciliation problem. When a regulator asks "who is accountable for verification?", the answer "D5 — which is C5 plus part of the Edge's Verification plus part of Governance" is exactly the multi-Guardian ambiguity the Option 1 red-team rated a problem (Finding 4: "Which Guardian halts what?"). The integration inherits that ambiguity and adds a second axis (Domain-name vs Engine/Core-implementation) along which it can be answered inconsistently.
  1. The framework forbids the very gap this risks. The Option 1 red-team's Finding 8.6 ("External legibility paradox") rated as a real incoherence any "gap between the real governance and the represented governance," because the framework forbids representational incoherence (§1.5 of the framework; Core Value: Coherence). Option 3's permanent hybrid is, structurally, two representations of one governance held in correspondence only by a hand-maintained table. The moment the table lags — and a fixed-vs-moving mapping with no maintenance protocol will lag — the named face says one thing (D2 owns coordination, or "the steward of the Core") while the real system does another (coordination is the Core; the Engine boundaries have moved; D5's verification is now three-quarters in C5). That is the represented-vs-real gap the design's own coherence value prohibits, reintroduced as a standing maintenance burden rather than a one-time risk.

Why it matters. Option 3's defence against "the legible face becomes a lie" is entirely "face and substrate are the same structure (the mapping table)" (§7 row 4; brief §4). If the table drifts, that defence collapses and the legible face is a lie — the precise failure mode the design is built to prevent, and an incoherence the framework treats as a first-order sin. The linchpin is a clerical artifact with no engineering behind its freshness, mapping a deliberately-fixed ontology onto a deliberately-moving substrate. That is not a reconciliation; it is a divergence with a maintenance to-do attached.

Severity: Major.

Fix.

  1. Engineer the mapping table as a system, not a document — apply Option 2's own C2 properties to it. Give it an owner (the D2/Core steward), a write-discipline (no Core module-boundary or Engine-boundary change merges without the corresponding mapping-row update in the same change), a freshness/staleness signal, and provenance (who changed which mapping when, and why). It is itself coordination-critical state; hold it to the standard the design holds all other coordination state.
  2. Add a periodic represented-vs-real governance audit as a named, dated Edge function (Option 3 already gestures at this in §7 row 4: "audit represented-vs-actual governance as a standing Edge function" — but it is unowned and uncadenced). Make it concrete: quarterly, the Governance function must reconstruct, from the live Core, who actually decides each "human-only" decision-class, and confirm it matches the named-Domain charter. A mismatch is logged as Alignment Debt and blocks further devolution until reconciled (mirroring the Option 1 red-team's floor-integrity fix, Finding 5).
  3. Resolve the one-to-many rows before deployment, not after. For D5↔(C5+Edge) and D6↔(E3+E5) and D7↔(Edge+Core-finance), state in one sentence each who the named accountable human is and which implementation components serve them, so the regulator-facing answer is unambiguous regardless of how the implementation evolves. The named ontology can stay fixed only if it is coarse enough to survive implementation churn; specify it at that altitude.

Evidence grade: Strong (the conceding parenthetical and the imperfect rows are in Option 3's own text; the coherence prohibition is in the framework and the prior red-team).

4. The phased migration is a mid-flight re-platforming that runs two coordination systems at once — and the riskiest phase (lifting coordination out of D2 into the Core) is the least specified

The claim. Option 3 §3 sequences Phase 1 ("Begin lifting coordination out of D2-the-Domain into the Core-the-substrate: the other Domains stop querying D2 as a department and start reading/writing shared state") and Phase 2 ("Re-cut the Domains into Engines… Do it on one venture first… then template"). The brief §3 calls this "the same first move" reaching "the destination" by "the path." The risk table (§7 row 3) addresses "Migration cost / double-running" with "keep the reversible rollback; migrate one venture first."

Steelman. "Migrate one venture first, instrument decision quality, pre-commit a rollback criterion" is genuinely good engineering discipline, borrowed correctly from Option 2's rollout (rollout-option2/ROLLOUT-PLAN.md Phase 3). Phasing a re-platforming is far better than a big-bang cutover, and the reversible rollback inherited from Option 1 is a real asset. The intent is sound.

Where it breaks. A re-platforming of the coordination substrate of a live organisation, while the organisation is operating, has a signature failure mode — two coordination systems disagreeing — that Option 3 acknowledges in a table row ("double-running") and then does not engineer against.

  1. Phase 1 is the dangerous window, and it is the least specified phase in the document. "Begin lifting coordination out of D2-the-Domain into the Core-the-substrate" means: for some period, some cross-Domain coordination runs through the old path (Domains querying D2-as-department / the human-curated memory + integrator) and some runs through the new path (Domains reading/writing the Core's shared state). That is two coordination systems live simultaneously over the same work. The Option 1 red-team's deepest finding (Finding 6) was that an under-specified coordination substrate drifts into "illegible, emergent, unaccountable coordination that no one designed and no one can see." Two partially-overlapping coordination systems is that risk doubled: a task can be coordinated one way by the old path and another way by the new, and the divergence is exactly the kind of silent mis-coordination that "memory describes disagreement; it cannot decide it" (Option 1 red-team Finding 2) predicts. Option 3 gives no cutover protocol for which task classes move when, no dual-write/dual-read reconciliation rule, no detection for "the two systems coordinated this differently," and no definition of "done" for a task class's migration beyond a single aggregate trigger ("cross-Domain coordination through shared state matches or beats the human-curated path on a measured task class").
  1. The trigger is a benchmark, not a cutover plan. "Matches or beats the human-curated path on a measured task class; Cognitive Overhead flat or falling" (§3 Phase 1) tells you when you are allowed to migrate a class, not how to run the two systems safely while you measure, nor how to roll a class back without leaving orphaned state in the Core. Benchmarking the new path against the old requires both to be live on the same traffic — which is the double-running risk, now mandated by the measurement method itself.
  1. Phase 2 re-cuts the org's units while the accountability face is supposed to stay fixed — a second simultaneous change. Phase 2 migrates Domains→Engines "by capability/module boundary." But §2/§6 insist the named Domains stay fixed forever as the accountability ontology. So Phase 2 changes the implementation units (Engines) underneath an unchanging name set (Domains) — which is the mapping-table-drift of Finding 3 happening as a deliberate phase, on a live venture, at the same time the coordination substrate is still settling from Phase 1. The document treats Phases 1 and 2 as sequential and clean; in practice the studio will likely be mid-Phase-1 (two coordination paths) when the first venture enters Phase 2 (units re-cut), because Phase 2 is explicitly "on one venture first" while the studio continues. That is three moving parts at once: old coordination path, new coordination path, and a re-cut unit boundary — over a 2–5 person team.

Why it matters. Re-platforming the coordination layer of a running institution is the highest-risk operation in the whole programme, and it is the one Option 3 specifies least. "Migrate one venture first" controls the Engine re-cut (Phase 2) but not the coordination lift (Phase 1), which happens at the studio level by definition (the studio's Domains stop querying D2 and start reading the Core). The document's single most under-engineered passage is the Phase-1 sentence that does the most dangerous thing.

Severity: Major.

Fix.

  1. Write a per-task-class cutover protocol for Phase 1, with: a single source of truth at any moment for each task class (a class is either on the human-curated path or on the Core, never silently both, except in an explicit, time-boxed shadow-comparison mode); a dual-run shadow mode where the Core coordinates in parallel but does not act, its output compared to the live human-curated path, before any class is flipped; and a reconciliation/alarm when the two disagree. This turns "double-running" from an acknowledged hazard into a controlled measurement.
  2. Define "migrated" and "rolled back" per class, including how Core state is cleaned up if a class is rolled back, so a reverted class does not leave the Core holding stale coordination assumptions other classes read.
  3. Sequence Phase 2 after the studio's Phase 1 is complete for the relevant task classes, or explicitly accept and resource the three-moving-parts window. Do not let "on one venture first" disguise that the coordination lift it depends on is a studio-level change that must be stable before the venture re-cut begins.

Evidence grade: Strong for the double-running risk (it is named in Option 3's own risk table and is a direct corollary of the prior red-team's Findings 2 and 6); theoretical for the three-moving-parts inference.

5. Scope creep — Option 3 is the everything-bagel: deploy the face, build the whole Core, wire four upgrades, keep a permanent hybrid, and aim at an unspecified Option 4 destination, for a 2–5 person team

The claim. Option 3 simultaneously commits to: (a) deploy Option 1's seven Domains now; (b) build Option 2's entire Core programme underneath; (c) wire in all four Option-4 "steal-ables" at Phase 1 (§4); (d) maintain the permanent hybrid with its canonical mapping table forever (§2); (e) migrate Domains→Engines (§3 Phase 2); and (f) "aim at the Option 4 destination" (§4 close: "make the eventual move toward Option 4… a continuation rather than a rebuild"; §9; founder decision 5). The brief §7 and the head-to-head §5 present this six-part programme as "the recommendation."

Steelman. Each individual item has a real rationale, and three of the four steal-ables are cheap and additive (the head-to-head argues they "improve whatever you build, at low cost"). Sequencing them into one narrative is more coherent than presenting six disconnected initiatives. And the founders did ask for an architecture that evolves toward autonomy, so naming a destination is responsive.

Where it breaks. The Option 1 red-team's sharpest single finding was an attention-arithmetic one: the framework's own binding constraint is that "human attention is the scarcest resource," and the org chart "spends the very resource it declares scarcest" (Finding 4). Option 3 is the most attention-expensive of all four options, and it never does the attention budget either.

  1. Six concurrent programmes against 2–5 humans, of whom realistically one or two hold most governance load (Option 1 red-team Finding 8.5: "largely Shayan"). Deploying a seven-Domain org and funding a multi-phase Core engineering build and implementing priced-attention + a reputation registry + rules-as-code + a pressure/selection signal and maintaining a canonical mapping table and planning a Domains→Engines migration and steering toward a market/genome destination is not a prioritised recommendation. It is a portfolio of every good idea in the entire option set, sequenced but not triaged. The framework's Cognitive Overhead KPI — "if it rises, FTLAB is drifting back to a conventional structure" (framework §5.2; Option 1 §5) — is the exact gauge this violates: a tiny team running six architectural programmes will spend all its attention on the architecture, not on the research and ventures the architecture exists to serve.
  1. Two of the four "low-cost" steal-ables are design-shaping, not additive — and Option 3 admits it. Option 3's own founder-decision 2 says "priced attention + reputation-as-autonomy are design-shaping" (§8). The Option 4 brief is blunter: a reputation registry needs "ground truth to price against; an early venture has almost none, and five cells + a dozen agents is not a market… likely underperforms at current scale" (case-for-option-4 §5.5). So Option 3 wires in, at Phase 1, two mechanisms that its source says do not work at FTLAB's current scale, and labels the bundle "low cost." Priced attention and reputation-as-earned-autonomy require an instrumented substrate (the Core) that, per Finding 1, does not yet exist during Phase 0–1 — so they are being scheduled before their prerequisite is built. That is not low cost; it is a dependency inversion.
  1. "Aim at the Option 4 destination" points at a target with no design. I verified this: there is no Option 4 design document. Option 4 exists only as an advocacy brief (positions/case-for-option-4-new-mode.md) which is explicit that the mode is "almost certainly wrong as a day-one build" and "a destination, not a starting point" (case-for-option-4 §5, §7), built on infrastructure that is "2025–26, mostly unproven at institutional scale, several blockchain-flavoured in ways that may not suit a DIFC-regulated, sovereign-facing institution" (§5.6). Option 3 commits to "aim at" and "bend toward" this destination (§4, §6, §9) and asks the founders to decide "how explicitly to aim at the Option 4 destination" (founder decision 5) — i.e., asks them to commit a heading toward an unspecified, self-described-as-immature, possibly-regulator-incompatible target. A recommendation cannot be steered toward a destination that has not been designed and that its own advocate disowns as a build.

Why it matters. The Option 1 red-team concluded the model was "over-scaled for its team." Option 3 is more over-scaled: it is Option 1's scope plus Option 2's build plus four Option-4 mechanisms plus a permanent reconciliation artifact plus a migration plus a heading toward an undesigned destination. Presenting all of it as "the recommendation" (§9) without a triage — what must happen in the first 90 days, what is deferred, what is merely a watching brief — converts a decision aid into a maximalist programme that a small team cannot absorb, and that maximises the one cost the framework says is binding.

Severity: Major.

Fix.

  1. Triage the six-part programme into Now / Next / Watch. Now (deployable, cheap, no Core dependency): deploy the legible face; start the artifact substrate; adopt only rules-as-code at the membrane (the one steal-able that is a genuine safety invariant and does not need market density). Next (gated on the Core existing and on scale): priced attention and reputation-as-autonomy (they need the instrumented Core and real ground truth — defer until both exist). Watch (north-star, no commitment): the Option 4 destination and the pressure/selection signal — held as a possibility, revisited when population density warrants, not "aimed at" until Option 4 is actually designed.
  2. Publish the attention budget the Option 1 red-team demanded, now for the integration. Name the human who owns each of the six programmes today; if any programme has no owner with spare attention, it is a backlog item, not a current commitment. Forbid "the founders, jointly" as the answer for more than one or two of the six.
  3. Do not ask the founders to "aim at" an undesigned destination. Either commission an Option 4 design (not just a brief) before treating it as a heading, or reframe founder-decision 5 as "preserve optionality toward a market/genome mode" with no architectural commitment until that mode is specified and shown to be DIFC/sovereign-compatible.

Evidence grade: Strong (the design-shaping admission and the "underperforms at current scale" warning are in Option 3's and Option 4's own text; the absence of an Option 4 design doc is confirmed on disk; the attention-arithmetic critique is inherited directly from the Option 1 red-team and applies a fortiori).

6. The "permanent hybrid forever" is not low-cost — it is permanent dual-maintenance plus an unreconciled two-devolution-sequence problem the design hand-waves

The claim. Option 3 §2 ("Why keep both faces forever"): "the named Domains cost almost nothing to retain (labels + charters + the Edge governance question applied per region)" and buy three permanent goods pure-Option-2 lacks. The brief §4 repeats: "the named layer costs almost nothing." The hybrid is the end-state, kept forever.

Steelman. The three goods are real and the framework genuinely needs them: external legibility / legal defensibility (a director must be a natural person; GDPR Art. 22 voids rubber-stamps — both cited correctly in §2), a stable accountability ontology, and a human-comprehensible onboarding/governance surface. Pure Option 2's "functions not personas" weakness is real (head-to-head matrix), and a named layer is the honest fix. Keeping a thin legible face over an efficient engine is defensible in principle.

Where it breaks. "Costs almost nothing" is asserted and is contradicted by the design's own moving parts.

  1. Dual-maintenance is the cost, and Finding 3 already priced it. Keeping both faces forever means maintaining, forever, the correspondence between a fixed named ontology and an evolving Core — i.e., the canonical mapping table, with the freshness/write-discipline/audit burden of Finding 3, in perpetuity. "Labels + charters" undercounts this: the cost is not the labels, it is the standing reconciliation between two representations every time the implementation moves. A permanent hybrid is a permanent mapping-drift risk and a permanent represented-vs-real audit obligation. That is the opposite of "almost nothing"; it is a forever-tax.
  1. The two-devolution-sequences reconciliation is hand-waved. Option 3 inherits two devolution logics: Option 1's "by systemic stakes" (Option 1 §6: build-first/devolve-last for the keystone; research first, oversight/sovereign-trust last) and Option 2's "by measured reliability per task class" (Option 2 §5). The brief §6 disposes of this in one clause: "they agree on order — systemic stakes sets the priority, measured reliability opens the gate; adopt both." But these are not obviously composable. Systemic stakes is a property of a Domain/function (the keystone is high-systemic-stakes regardless of how reliable it gets). Measured reliability is a property of a task class (a class promotes when its verifiers hold). Option 3 runs both taxonomies at once — Domains (for accountability) and task classes (for coordination/devolution) — so it must reconcile a function-level priority order with a task-class-level gate, across the mapping table, continuously. "Adopt both" is a slogan, not a mechanism: it does not say what happens when a high-systemic-stakes Domain contains a high-reliability task class (does the Domain's systemic stakes veto the class's reliability gate, or not?). This is the same one-to-many problem as Finding 3, now in the devolution machinery, and it is unaddressed.
  1. The permanent floor's separation-of-powers problem (Finding 7) is also permanent in the hybrid. Because the named Cortex is kept forever as the accountability face, the unsolved 2–5-person separation-of-powers (next finding) is not a transitional embarrassment but a permanent structural feature of the end-state — the hybrid bakes it in rather than growing out of it.

Why it matters. "Keep both forever, it's almost free" is the justification for the entire end-state design choice (permanent hybrid over eventual collapse to pure Option 2 — founder decision 4). If the true cost is permanent dual-maintenance + a permanent unreconciled two-sequence devolution problem + a permanently-baked-in separation-of-powers gap, then the choice "permanent hybrid vs collapse to Option 2" is a real trade-off the founders should make with eyes open, not a free lunch. The design currently hides the recurring cost behind "labels + charters."

Severity: Minor. (The hybrid is genuinely defensible and the goods are real; the flaw is the understated cost and the unreconciled second sequence, not the choice itself. It is rated Minor rather than Major because, unlike Findings 1–4, it does not threaten the design's viability — it threatens the honesty of its cost accounting and one mechanism.)

Fix.

  1. Price the hybrid honestly: state that the permanent goods are bought at the cost of a permanent mapping-table-maintenance discipline and a permanent represented-vs-real audit (Finding 3's fixes, made standing), and that this is a deliberate trade vs. eventually collapsing to pure Option 2. Let the founders choose with the real cost visible.
  2. Specify the two-sequence reconciliation as a rule, not a slogan. State the precedence explicitly: e.g., "measured reliability opens the gate for a task class; systemic stakes set a floor below which no class in a high-systemic-stakes Domain promotes regardless of measured reliability, until the Domain-level oversight is externalised." Make "adopt both" into an actual decision procedure at the Domain×task-class intersection.

Evidence grade: Strong (the "costs almost nothing" claim and the "adopt both" clause are in the design's and brief's own text; the two-sequence non-composability is a direct reading of Option 1 §6 vs Option 2 §5).

7. Guardian / separation-of-powers is inherited unsolved — and the permanent hybrid makes the unsolved version permanent

The claim. Option 3 §7 (row 5): "Founders hold it jointly under a logged COI protocol now; dated externalisation trigger; track as Alignment Debt. (Inherited from both options; not created by integrating.)" Phase 1 (§3): "Move the Cortex toward separation-of-powers (Direction / Verification / Governance held by different humans), and set a dated externalisation trigger for the Guardian halt-right." Founder decision 3 asks the founders to confirm this.

Steelman. This is the most honest part of the document. It correctly inherits the Option 1 red-team's Critical Finding 3 ("Guardian independence is asserted but structurally impossible at current scale") and the head-to-head's framing ("still unresolved in every option at 2–5 people"), it does not pretend to have solved it, it commits to a dated externalisation trigger (an improvement on Option 1 v0.3's undated "as the team grows"), and it logs the gap as Alignment Debt. The "inherited, not created by integrating" claim is true — integration does not make this worse.

Where it breaks. Two points, one inherited and one integration-specific.

  1. Inherited and still unfixed: at 2–5 people, separation-of-powers is arithmetically impossible, and Phase 1's "move toward" it does not change the arithmetic. The Option 1 red-team's Finding 3 stands undisturbed: "the same handful of people set intent, allocate capital, and guard values… 'Independent of P&L' with one principal is a category error, not a design." Option 3 inherits this verbatim and its Phase 1 instruction ("held by different humans") presupposes enough humans to hold three functions in tension — which 2–5 people, realistically 2 founders, do not have. Option 2's separation-of-powers (different humans, in tension) is more demanding than Option 1's joint-Guardian-with-COI, and Option 3 adopts the Option 2 version (§3 Phase 1) without adding the humans to staff it. So Option 3's separation-of-powers is, if anything, harder to satisfy than Option 1's, at the same headcount. The "dated externalisation trigger" is the right fix — but it is a promise, and until the date arrives the gap is live, exactly as in Option 1.
  1. Integration-specific: the permanent hybrid makes the gap permanent. Because §2 keeps the named Cortex forever as the accountability face, the 2–5-person separation-of-powers problem is no longer just a near-term embarrassment to be grown out of — it is a feature of the end-state. In pure Option 2 with a growing Edge, separation-of-powers becomes real as the Edge grows. In Option 3's permanent hybrid, the named Cortex/Edge is kept small and legible by design ("the smallest legitimate human Edge/Cortex," §6), so the design's own end-state holds the human accountability layer at the size where separation-of-powers is hardest. The design must therefore lean entirely on externalisation (an independent board member / reviewer with a real halt-right) as the permanent answer — which it gestures at but does not commit to as a permanent end-state requirement, only as a growth-triggered one.

Why it matters. The Guardian is the mechanism the whole framework relies on to keep devolution from eroding values (Option 1 red-team Finding 3: "If it is not real, the 'permanent accountability floor' is decorative, and the model's central safety claim is unfunded"). Option 3 keeps the floor permanent and keeps the human layer permanently small, which means the externalised Guardian is not a transitional crutch but a permanent structural necessity — and the design treats it as optional/growth-triggered.

Severity: Major. (Inherited Critical from Option 1, slightly mitigated by the dated trigger and the honest logging, but made permanent by the hybrid end-state — net Major.)

Fix.

  1. Make the externalised Guardian a permanent end-state requirement, not a growth trigger. State plainly: because the hybrid keeps the human Edge permanently small, genuine separation-of-powers requires an external halt-right holder (independent director / sovereign-acceptable reviewer) as a standing feature of the end-state, not a milestone to reach and forget. Name the trigger date and name it as permanent thereafter.
  2. Adopt the Option 1 red-team's discretion-free "never" lines now (Finding 3 fix / head-to-head Option 1 fix: "make the 'never' rules discretion-free so they bind a conflicted Guardian"). Until an external Guardian exists, the only real protection is bright-line rules that bind without the conflicted founder's judgment. This is the interim mechanism Option 3 should commit to explicitly, not leave to the inherited COI protocol.
  3. Tie the Guardian gap to the devolution gate (consistent with Finding 6). No high-systemic-stakes Domain promotes toward autonomy until the external halt-right is in place — making the unsolved separation-of-powers a brake on devolution rather than a logged-and-ignored debt.

Evidence grade: Strong (inherited directly from the Option 1 red-team Finding 3 and the head-to-head; the permanence point is a direct reading of Option 3 §2 and §6).

8. "The recommendation" lets the founders defer the one real decision — which primitive leads — and commit to the most complex path by default

The claim. Option 3 §9 ("Why this is the recommendation") and the head-to-head §5 present Option 3 as the integrated recommendation, dissolving the Option-1-vs-Option-2 choice into sequencing: "Most of the apparent 'Option 1 vs 2' choice dissolves into sequencing" (head-to-head §6). Option 3's founder decisions (§8) are about how to execute the integration (commit to the Core-from-day-zero? adopt the four upgrades? dated Guardian trigger? confirm the permanent hybrid? how explicitly to aim at Option 4?) — not whether to integrate.

Steelman. It is genuinely true that the artifact substrate must be built regardless of which face runs on top (head-to-head §6 decision 1 is real and correct), and that some convergence between a legible face and an engineered substrate is where all four advocates land. To that extent, "the choice is more about sequencing than a hard fork" is a fair reading, and presenting an integrated path is a service.

Where it breaks. "Dissolves into sequencing" is the move by which the hardest decision is avoided rather than made — and the default it leaves standing is the most complex, most expensive, least-proven path.

  1. There is a real fork, and Option 3 buries it. Option 2's advocacy states the fork plainly: Option 1's primitive (a department chart of agent-managers) is "not fixable without becoming Option 2 — you can't refactor your way out of the wrong primitive" (head-to-head Position 2). Option 4's advocacy states a deeper fork: both 1 and 2 "quietly rebuild the central coordinator the lineage-break thesis says is dissolving" (case-for-option-4 §1; head-to-head Position 4). These are not sequencing questions — they are "which primitive leads: the named department, the engineered Core, or the constituted market?" Option 3 answers "all of them, in order, forever" — which is not a resolution of the fork but a refusal to choose, packaged as a synthesis. The named department leads the accountability face (permanently), the Core leads coordination (after a long build), and the market leads the destination (undesigned). A founder who adopts Option 3 has not decided which primitive is primary; they have committed to maintain all three.
  1. The default left standing is the maximal path. Because Option 3 is framed as "the recommendation" and its founder-decisions are about execution detail, the path of least resistance for a founder is to say "yes, integrate" — thereby committing, by default, to the six-part everything-bagel of Finding 5 (deploy + build the whole Core + four upgrades + permanent hybrid + migration + aim at Option 4). The genuinely cheaper and more focused options — "ship Option 1, harvest Option 2 as a v0.4 backlog" (head-to-head Position 1's actual verdict) or "build the Core, populate Engines from Option 1's charters, skip the permanent dual-face" (Position 2's verdict) — are more decisive and less complex, and Option 3's framing makes them look like failures to integrate rather than legitimate choices. The complexity is thereby selected by default, not by decision.
  1. The framework's own posture argues against this. The founders asked for "conviction without rigidity" and "dynamic over rigid" (founder responses, Additional Notes 4–5) — but also for focus: attention is the binding constraint, ventures are the research, and FTLAB is "learning in action" on a small team. A recommendation that commits a 2–5 person team to three primitives and six programmes is the opposite of the focused, attention-conserving posture the founders described. The honest recommendation would force the primitive choice, not dissolve it.

Why it matters. The Option 1 red-team's verdict turned on whether the model "lets the founders avoid the real choice." Option 3, as "the recommendation," does exactly that at the level of the whole option set: it lets the founders avoid deciding which primitive leads by offering to run all three, and it makes the most complex path the default yes. For an attention-scarce team, defaulting into maximal complexity is the single most expensive mistake available, and the framing nudges toward it.

Severity: Major.

Fix.

  1. Reframe Option 3 from "the recommendation" to "the maximal integration option, whose cost is honestly stated," and force the primitive decision. Present three genuinely distinct, decisive paths to the founders: (A) Lead with the legible department — ship Option 1, treat the Core as a funded v0.4 backlog, adopt only rules-as-code now, defer the rest (lowest complexity, Option 1's primitive critique accepted). (B) Lead with the engineered Core — build the Core first, populate Engines from Option 1's charters, use named seats only as the thin legal terminus, not a permanent parallel ontology (Option 2's path; no permanent dual-maintenance). (C) The full integration — Option 3 as written, with the costs of Findings 1–7 made explicit. Make the founders choose A, B, or C, not "yes."
  2. State the one decision that does not dissolve into sequencing: is the primary, leading primitive the named department (legibility-first), the engineered Core (coordination-first), or a constituted market (incentive-first, and not yet buildable)? Everything else is downstream of that, and the founders should answer it explicitly before committing to a path.
  3. Default to the least-complex path that meets the binding constraints, and require an explicit, attention-budgeted justification for choosing the more complex one — consistent with "attention is the scarcest resource."

Evidence grade: Strong (the "dissolves into sequencing" framing is in the head-to-head's own text; the buried fork is stated by Options 2 and 4's own advocates; the attention constraint is the framework's).

What survives the critique

Several elements of Option 3 are genuinely robust and should be kept even if the integration as a whole is re-scoped:

  • The core observation that Option 1 and Option 2 describe one structure at two altitudes is largely correct and valuable. The mapping (Cortex↔Edge, D1↔E1, D3↔E2, D6↔E3+E5, and especially D2 ↔ the Core minus the engineering) is a real insight, well-supported by both source documents, and it is the most useful single contribution of the whole option exercise. The integration's diagnosis is sound; it is the cost accounting and sequencing that overclaim.
  • "Re-seat Option 1's accountability layer on Option 2's engineered Core instead of on the hand-wavy coordination in D2" is the right architectural move. This is precisely the fix the Option 1 red-team prescribed for its Critical finding (specify D2 as a coordination system; build it to the freshness/conflict-merge/provenance/out-of-band-verification spec). Option 3 correctly identifies that Option 2's Core is that specification. Keep this — just stop claiming it is free or already done.
  • The hard gate ("until the Core passes verification, D2 stays human-curated") is the correct safety behaviour. It prevents the silent-year-two failure of a trusted-but-half-built Core. The flaw is not the gate; it is that the gate is the admission that the engineered coordination is unavailable during the build, which the "deployable now / closes the Critical finding" headline contradicts.
  • The instinct to keep a named, legible accountability face is right and faithful to the framework and the founders. A director must be a natural person; GDPR Art. 22 voids rubber-stamps; sovereigns and regulators need named answerable humans (founder responses Q1, Q6; framework Coherence value). Pure Option 2's "functions not personas" is a real gap, and a named layer is the honest fix. The flaw is the understated permanent cost of maintaining the correspondence, not the layer itself.
  • Rules-as-code at the membrane (steal-able #3) is a genuine, scale-independent improvement and should be adopted now regardless of which path is chosen — machine-enforced non-negotiable Values are a real safety invariant that does not depend on market density or an instrumented Core.
  • The honest inheritance of the Guardian problem (logging it as Alignment Debt, committing to a dated externalisation trigger) is intellectually honest and an improvement on Option 1 v0.3's undated version. Keep the honesty; make the external Guardian permanent (Finding 7).
  • "Prove it on one venture first, instrument decision quality, pre-commit a rollback criterion" is correct engineering discipline, borrowed accurately from Option 2's rollout. Keep it — and extend the same discipline to the Phase-1 coordination lift, which currently lacks it (Finding 4).

Verdict

Salvageable with significant changes — the diagnosis is excellent and two or three core moves are right, but the central "one build, deployable now, closes the Critical finding" claim does not hold as stated, the cost accounting understates the real burden, and presenting the maximal path as "the recommendation" lets the founders default into the most complex option without making the one decision that matters.

Option 3 is not flawed at the foundation in the sense of a wrong premise: its observation that Options 1 and 2 are one structure at two altitudes is correct and useful, and its prescription — re-seat the legible accountability layer on an engineered Core — is exactly the fix the Option 1 red-team demanded. But its load-bearing sales claim (Finding 1) composes two separately-true half-truths into a falsehood: it is deployable now as a legible face running on Option 1's still-Critical human-curated coordination, and it closes the Critical finding only when a long, unhardened, multi-phase Core build succeeds — not, as the headline says, simultaneously and for the price of one build. Stripped of that conflation, Option 3 is honestly described as: Option 1's deployable skeleton, plus 100% of Option 2's inherited Core programme, plus an integration-specific dual-maintenance and re-platforming cost (Findings 3, 4, 6), aimed at an undesigned destination (Finding 5), with Option 1's primitive critique retained through the interim and Option 2's central-fragility worsened (Finding 2) — over a 2–5 person team that the framework's own binding constraint says cannot afford it (Findings 5, 8). It is salvageable because the fixes are concrete, the diagnosis survives, and the honesty the design already shows in places (the hard gate, the logged Guardian debt) can be extended to the claims that currently overreach.

The 3–5 changes that would most strengthen it:

  1. Tell the truth about the timeline and the build (Finding 1). Replace "one build, not two / deployable now / closes the Critical finding" with: deploys the legible face now (carrying Option 1's Critical keystone risk in the interim), builds Option 2's Core as a separate multi-phase programme, and closes the Critical finding only when that Core passes verification. Put a dated, resourced budget and an abort/timebox on the Core programme, and a risk register for the interim window during which the institution runs on un-engineered coordination.
  1. Replace "adds no new fatal risk" with a real risk ledger, and red-team the Core (Finding 2). State, per inherited flaw, whether integration cancels / reduces / retains / worsens it — including that Option 2's central single-point-of-failure is worsened by dissolving Option 1's Domain firewalls — and add the three integration-born risks. Commission the Core's adversarial review as a Phase-0 gate (the head-to-head's own recommendation), and specify a degraded-mode coordination fallback so a Core drift cannot silently mis-coordinate everything.
  1. Engineer the canonical mapping table as coordination-critical state and audit represented-vs-real governance (Finding 3). Give the table an owner, a write-discipline (no Core/Engine boundary change merges without the mapping-row update), freshness signals, and a quarterly Governance audit that reconstructs from the live Core who actually decides each human-only class — with mismatches logged as Alignment Debt that brakes devolution. Resolve the one-to-many rows (D5, D6, D7) into one unambiguous named-accountability sentence each, before deployment.
  1. Triage the everything-bagel and stop aiming at an undesigned destination (Finding 5). Sort the six programmes into Now (legible face + artifact substrate + rules-as-code), Next (priced attention + reputation, gated on the Core existing and on real ground truth — both of which their own sources say are absent at current scale), and Watch (the Option 4 destination and pressure/selection signal — optionality only, no architectural commitment until Option 4 is actually designed and shown DIFC/sovereign-compatible). Publish the attention budget; forbid "the founders jointly" as the owner of more than one or two programmes.
  1. Force the primitive decision instead of dissolving it, and make the external Guardian permanent (Findings 7, 8). Present three decisive paths — lead with the legible department (lowest complexity), lead with the engineered Core, or the full integration (with Findings 1–7's costs made explicit) — and require the founders to choose which primitive leads, not merely "yes, integrate." Because the hybrid keeps the human Edge permanently small, commit the externalised independent Guardian halt-right as a permanent end-state requirement (not a growth trigger), adopt discretion-free "never" lines in the interim, and tie the Guardian gap to the devolution brake.

Held in the framework's own spirit (conviction without rigidity, attention as the scarcest resource): Option 3's insight is worth keeping and its engineering prescription is right — but it should be re-scoped from a maximal "do everything, forever, now" recommendation into an honestly-costed, triaged path that forces the one decision (which primitive leads) it currently lets the founders avoid.
