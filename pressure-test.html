Option 1 · red-team

Pressure-Test — Option 1

The adversarial red-team that hardened the design — three Critical findings and their fixes.

Option 123 min readClassification · Strategic / InternalSource · ftlab-org-architecture-pressure-test-v0.1.md
In brief

An independent adversarial review of the early seven-Domain model. Its findings (memory-as-coordinator asserted not engineered; coordination-without-a-chain risk; Guardian independence at tiny scale; over-scaling) were folded into v0.3 and shape every later option.

Key takeaways
  • Critical: “memory-as-coordinator is asserted, not engineered” — fixed by the Core.
  • Critical: an independent Guardian is hard at 2–5 people — logged as Alignment Debt, externalised later.
  • Major: a half-built coordination substrate fails silently and late (“authoritative at six months, wrong at year two”).
  • Verdict at the time: salvageable with significant changes — which v0.3 made.

This is an adversarial review commissioned by the author of the model under review, who explicitly asked to be challenged rather than reassured. Its job is to find where the model breaks, state why that matters, rate severity, and recommend a concrete fix. It steelmans objections before answering them. It does not relitigate whether the AI-Born book is correct — that is conceded as direction of travel. It attacks the model itself: its core distinction, its keystone mechanism, its governance claims, its fidelity to FTLAB's own framework and founders, and the failure modes it does not address. Where the model is genuinely robust, that is recorded in "What survives the critique." The verdict is at the end.

0. Summary of the model under review

The model proposes that a small human Cortex governs ten agent-run Domains (each = a Head agent + agent team + an assigned Architect/Intent-Setter/Guardian Triumvirate + a Charter bounding authority), coordinated not by a management chain but by shared institutional memory (Domain 2). The org "radiates, does not descend." Authority devolves over time by widening each Domain's Charter, paced by evidence, with a permanent human accountability floor justified by legitimacy rather than capability. The pattern federates to each portfolio venture (its own cortex, its own agents). The single most important construction claim is: ten autonomous Domains can cohere without a chain because shared memory routes what managers used to route, and accountability/judgment stay human.

The model is intelligent, internally careful about marking its claims, and substantially faithful to the framework's vocabulary. The findings below are where it nonetheless breaks or overclaims.

1. The core distinction (routing vs accountability) papers over entangled cases

The claim. Borrowing from Block: hierarchy did two jobs — routing information and locating accountability/judgment. AI now does the first via a shared world model; the second stays human. So: automate routing (Domain 2), keep judgment in the Cortex.

Steelman of the model. For a large share of knowledge work this is real. Much of what middle managers did was move information, schedule, and reconcile — and a good shared memory plus capable agents genuinely absorbs that. The distinction is also clarifying: it names which part of hierarchy is safe to remove.

Where it breaks. The distinction treats routing and judgment as separable layers. In FTLAB's actual work they are frequently the same act. Three concrete cases:

  1. Routing under ambiguity is itself a judgment. Deciding which Domain owns a novel problem — e.g., a sovereign partner asks for something that is simultaneously a consulting engagement (Domain 8), a venture origination (Domain 3), an IP-tier question (Domain 6), and a thesis-validation question (Domain 1) — is not a lookup. It is a framing decision that determines the answer. "The shared world model routes it" assumes the routing target is knowable from the memory; for genuinely novel cross-Domain items it is not, and the framing is the high-stakes call. The model has automated exactly the part that is hardest.
  2. Synthesis is routing that creates judgment. Domain 1→2 the moment a finding is "admitted as validated," a definition changes, or two authoritative documents conflict. The model itself concedes these are escalations — but that concession quietly admits that the highest-value routing events are also the judgment events. The clean split holds only for low-stakes, high-volume traffic; it dissolves precisely where coordination matters most.
  3. Prioritisation is routing-as-judgment. Allocating scarce human attention across ten Domains (which escalation do I look at first?) is a routing act whose criterion is judgment. The model gives no mechanism for this routing — see Finding 6.

Why it matters. The model's entire load-bearing move ("we can drop the management chain because AI does routing") is sound only for the easy 80%. The hard 20% — novel, cross-Domain, contested, ambiguous — is where flat orgs historically failed, and it is precisely the cases the routing/judgment split fails to separate. The model has not retired the management function; it has hidden it inside the words "escalate" and "shared memory."

Severity: Major.

Fix. Reframe the distinction honestly: routing of legible, precedented traffic is automatable; routing of novel/ambiguous/cross-Domain traffic is a judgment act and must be explicitly owned. Name the owner of cross-Domain routing (a Cortex role — effectively a chief-of-staff/integration function; see Finding 6). Do not claim the chain is gone; claim it has shrunk to the irreducible set of framing decisions and named that set.

2. KEYSTONE RISK — "memory-as-coordinator" is under-specified and is the single biggest point of failure

The claim. Coordination runs through Domain 2's shared, machine-readable, continuously-current institutional memory instead of a management chain. "Coordinating ten agent-run functions through shared memory is impossible if the memory is thin," so build Domain 2 first.

Steelman. A genuinely excellent, current, queryable institutional memory would do real coordinating work, and the instinct to build it first is correct. Shared world models do reduce coordination cost. This is the most defensible part of the design's logic.

Where it breaks. The model asserts the mechanism but never specifies what makes memory coordinating rather than merely recorded. A document store is not a coordinator. Coordination requires the memory to do things the model never operationalises:

  • Currency. Coordination-by-memory fails the instant the memory lags reality. Ten Domains acting continuously generate state faster than any "knowledge-manager + technical-writer" can curate. The model gives no write-discipline, no freshness SLA, no staleness detection. Stale memory is worse than no memory, because Domains will act on it confidently.
  • Conflict resolution. The model concedes Domain 2 must "resolve genuine conflicts between authoritative documents" and escalate when it cannot — but a coordinator's defining job is conflict resolution, and the model offloads the hard cases back to humans without saying who or how. When Domain 3 (build fast) and Domain 4 (rigour gate) read the same memory and reach opposite conclusions, the memory does not adjudicate; it just holds both. Memory describes disagreement; it cannot decide it. That is the function a chain provided.
  • Contestation / authority. Who decides what is "authoritative"? The corpus already contains live contradictions the model itself lists (the ratio "5:100" vs "1:500" vs "1:100–1:10,000"; "Five Planes" used two incompatible ways — and note the project's own CLAUDE.md defines the Five Planes as Strategy/Architecture/Governance/Operations/Intelligence while the framework defines them as Data/Model/Agent/Orchestration/Actuation). If the institution cannot keep its own foundational terms consistent across two documents today, "coordinate ten autonomous Domains through shared memory" is aspirational, not operational. The keystone is being asked to bear weight the institution has not yet demonstrated it can bear at trivial scale.
  • Gaming / Goodhart. Agents optimised against a memory will write to look coordinated (citing the right decision logs, matching the glossary) without being coordinated. The model has no integrity check distinguishing genuine alignment from recorded alignment — which is the agentic analogue of the Alignment Debt the framework already warns about, but applied to the coordination substrate itself.
  • Single point of failure. The model makes coordination depend entirely on one Domain, then schedules that Domain among the first to devolve to agents (§5). So the institution's entire coordination capacity becomes an agent-run system, early, with the thinnest human oversight — and if it drifts, every other Domain silently mis-coordinates. There is no second coordination path.

Why it matters. This is the hinge of the whole model. If memory-as-coordinator is hand-wavy, the model reduces to "ten autonomous teams with shared docs and no boss," which is the flat-org failure mode the model claims to avoid (see Finding 7). Everything else in the design is downstream of this working.

Severity: Critical.

Fix.

  1. Specify Domain 2 as a coordination system, not a library: define (a) a write-discipline every Domain must follow (decisions logged in a structured, queryable form within a fixed window), (b) a freshness/staleness signal surfaced to consumers, (c) an explicit conflict-resolution protocol with a named human adjudicator of last resort, and (d) a provenance/authority model (what makes a document canonical, who can canonicalise).
  2. Prove it at current scale before betting the org on it. Resolve the listed corpus contradictions (ratio, Five Planes, CLAUDE.md vs framework) as the first test of the memory. If the memory cannot hold three definitions straight, it cannot coordinate ten Domains.
  3. Devolve Domain 2 last, not first. Build the content first; keep human ownership of the coordination substrate among the longest, because it is the system every other Domain trusts blindly. The model's "build first" and "devolve early" are in tension; split them.

3. Guardian independence is asserted but structurally impossible at current scale

The claim. Domain 4 holds an institution-wide Guardian that is "structurally independent of profit-and-loss" and can never be "overridden on a values breach by a revenue consideration."

Steelman. The principle is exactly right and is core framework doctrine (New Triumvirate, §2.3; Stewardship Over Extraction, §2.8). An independent halt-authority is what makes "values-conscious architecture" more than a slogan.

Where it breaks. FTLAB is 2–5 humans. The model's own §7 admits "today Shayan holds Guardian-type and operating roles together" and calls fixing this "the most consequential near-term human decision." But the model does not actually resolve it — it defers it to the founders and proceeds as if independence exists. At this size, structural independence is arithmetically impossible: the same handful of people set intent, allocate capital, and guard values. A Guardian who is also the Managing Partner cannot halt a revenue-driving venture against his own P&L interest without halting himself. "Independent of P&L" with one principal is a category error, not a design.

Worse, the model then delegates the Guardian to an agent Domain while insisting the human accountability floor holds — but a Guardian agent inherits its values from the same humans whose conflicts it is meant to check, and cannot exercise the very thing the floor is for (legitimacy before a regulator/court). So neither the human nor the agent version delivers genuine independence at this scale.

Why it matters. The Guardian is the mechanism the framework relies on to keep the whole model coherent (it is the answer to "what stops devolution from eroding values?"). If it is not real, the "permanent accountability floor" (Finding 5) is decorative, and the model's central safety claim is unfunded.

Severity: Critical.

Fix. Stop claiming independence the size cannot support. Either: (a) externalise the Guardian — an independent board member, an external ethics/audit reviewer, or a sovereign-partner-acceptable third party with a real, exercisable halt right; or (b) name an explicit conflict-of-interest protocol for the founder-as-Guardian case (e.g., pre-committed bright-line "never" rules that bind without discretionary judgment, plus mandatory external review when a values/revenue conflict arises). State plainly that true structural independence arrives only when headcount or an external party makes it possible, and treat the interim as a known, logged risk — not a solved problem.

4. Ten Domains is too many to govern, and several have no real human owner

The claim. Ten functions, each "ownable," each mapping to a C-suite equivalent, each governed by an assigned Triumvirate.

Steelman. The ten are derived from real work, not invented, and most map to existing agents. The cut is more disciplined than a generic C-suite copy.

Where it breaks.

  • Attention arithmetic contradicts the framework's own binding constraint. The model's foundational principle (from the framework) is that human attention is the scarcest resource. Ten Domains, each with a Triumvirate of three roles = up to 30 role-slots, against 2–5 humans. Even collapsing roles, each human must hold the Architect/Intent-Setter/Guardian load for several Domains simultaneously. The model spends the very resource it declares scarcest, and never does the attention budget. This is the framework's Cognitive Overhead metric being violated by the org chart that invokes it.
  • Domains with no real owner. By the model's own devolution map, Domain 10 (People/Culture/Apprenticeship) "must never be automated away" because it "produces the humans the model depends on" — yet with 2–5 humans there is no one to run an apprenticeship pipeline, and "growing the next humans" presupposes a teaching capacity the institution does not have. Domain 7 (Capital/Ops) and Domain 9 (Platform) are flagged as genuine gaps with "new" agents and no current human owner. A Domain whose human owner is "TBD / a founder part-time" is not governed; it is nominally chartered.
  • Overlap / contested boundaries. Guardian sits in Domain 4 but the framework's Triumvirate also puts a Guardian inside every Domain and Domain 6 is given "a Guardian role" too — so "the Guardian" is simultaneously institution-wide (Domain 4) and distributed (every Domain) and legal-specific (Domain 6). Which Guardian halts what? Domain 8 (Network/sovereign) and Domain 3 (venture origination) overlap precisely where consulting-becomes-venture (the framework's core flywheel mechanic) — the highest-value, most-contested boundary, left unarbitrated.

Why it matters. Ten is a number that looks like good organisational design (clean C-suite mapping) but is governed by a team that cannot staff the governance. The mismatch reintroduces the exact coordination overhead the model claims to remove — and the un-owned Domains are where drift will start.

Severity: Major.

Fix.

  1. Collapse to the smallest set a single-digit team can actually govern — propose ~5–6 Domains for now (e.g., merge 4+6 into one Integrity/Guardianship Domain; fold 7 and 10's running into a single Institution/Operations Domain until headcount justifies splitting; keep Research(1), Knowledge(2), Venture(3), Network(8) distinct). Let Domains split as humans are added — same federation logic, applied internally.
  2. Publish a one-line attention budget: for each Domain, name the human who holds each Triumvirate role today, and forbid any role being held by "nobody." If a role has no human, the Domain does not exist yet — it is a backlog item.
  3. Disambiguate the Guardian: one institution-wide halt authority; Domain-level "guardian" renamed to avoid collision (e.g., "compliance check"), so "who halts what" is answerable in one sentence as the framework demands.

5. The devolution sequence is roughly right, but the "permanent floor" is convenient and erodes in practice

The claim. Devolve high-volume/low-stakes first (Research, Knowledge), trust/ethics/accountability last (Quality, Governance, Network). The floor is permanent because it rests on legitimacy, not capability: a regulator/court/sovereign needs a nameable human, and values must be authored by humans.

Steelman. The "legitimacy not capability" argument is genuinely strong and is the best single idea in the document. It correctly identifies that even a fully capable agent institution needs a human answerable in law and trust — that is not a transitional crutch, it is structural. The sequence (volume-low-stakes first) is also sound.

Where it breaks.

  • The sequence contradicts the keystone. §5 puts Knowledge (Domain 2) in the first/furthest devolution tier. But Domain 2 is the coordination substrate the whole model depends on (Finding 2). Devolving the coordinator early, while every other Domain still leans on it, is the riskiest possible order. The sequence optimises per-Domain stakes but ignores systemic stakes — Knowledge has low per-action stakes and maximal systemic stakes. This is an internal contradiction between §3.1/§4 (keystone) and §5 (sequence).
  • The floor erodes by rubber-stamping, and the model has no defence against it. "Legitimacy requires a nameable human" guarantees a human signs, not that a human judges. As agents handle more, the human's ability to meaningfully review what they sign decays (they lack the context, the volume is too high, the agent output is fluent and persuasive). The framework's own Cognitive Overhead and Alignment Debt metrics predict exactly this: the floor stays nominally in place while becoming a formality. The model invokes these metrics but does not connect them to floor-erosion or set a trigger.
  • "Values authored by humans" is asserted, not protected. Once agents draft the values, the policies, the charters, and the memory humans read to decide, human "authorship" can become human ratification of agent-proposed values. The model has no mechanism ensuring values originate from human judgment rather than being laundered through the agent layer.

Why it matters. The floor is the model's answer to "doesn't this just become a no-human org that fails the legitimacy test?" If the floor can erode silently to a signature, the model's safety guarantee is hollow exactly when it matters (under regulatory or sovereign scrutiny, after years of devolution).

Severity: Major.

Fix.

  1. Re-order: move Knowledge/coordination-substrate to the late tier (build content early, devolve control late). Devolution sequence should weight systemic stakes, not just per-action stakes.
  2. Define a floor-integrity test: a periodic check that the accountable human can, in fact, reconstruct and defend the reasoning behind decisions they are signing (sample-audit "explain this decision without the agent's summary"). Tie a rising Cognitive Overhead / Alignment Debt reading to a mandatory slowdown of devolution, not just monitoring.
  3. Protect authorship: require that Values-level constraints and Charter "never" lines are human-originated and human-changeable only, with agent proposals explicitly logged as proposals, never as adopted values.

6. Coordination without a chain does not prevent drift — it recreates the flat-org failure mode

The claim. Ten autonomous Domains coordinated by shared memory plus one repeated governance question ("who holds Architect/Intent-Setter/Guardian, and are they in tension?") cohere "without rebuilding bureaucracy." Citing Zappos/GitHub reversals, the model claims FTLAB is small enough to sit in the size range where legible flat coordination reliably works.

Steelman. The model does not naively claim flatness; it explicitly concedes flat orgs revert because "removing legible authority just creates illegible authority," and it leans on small human headcount as the escape. That is more honest than most flat-org advocacy.

Where it breaks — this is the deepest structural objection. The model draws the wrong boundary around "small." The flat-org evidence is about coordinating humans. The model then asserts FTLAB stays in the safe zone because its human count is small — while adding a large agent population that does the actual work and the actual coordinating. The coordination complexity of an organisation is a function of the number of acting agents and the interactions among them, not the number of humans who sign off. Ten Domains each running an agent swarm, all acting continuously, is not a small coordination problem; it is a large one wearing a small-headcount costume. The human-count argument is a sleight of hand: it answers "can a few humans get along without a chain?" (yes) while the real question is "can hundreds of agents across ten Domains avoid conflict, drift, and duplicated/contradictory action without a coordinating authority?" (the model never addresses this).

And the model's own evidence cuts against it. Zappos/GitHub reverted because informal hierarchy fills the vacuum. In an agent population, the vacuum is filled not by an informal human leader but by whatever the memory and the most assertive agents default to — an emergent, illegible coordination that no one designed and no one can see. The single "governance question" is a diagnostic, not a coordinator: asking "are the three roles in tension?" tells you whether a Domain is well-governed; it does nothing to resolve a live conflict between two Domains acting now.

Why it matters. This is the model's central claim turned on its head: the architecture markets itself as "neither pyramid nor flat collective," but having removed the chain and under-specified the memory (Finding 2), it is a flat collective — of agents — and inherits the flat collective's known failure: drift into illegible, emergent, unaccountable coordination. The added agent population reintroduces exactly the complexity the human-count argument waves away.

Severity: Critical.

Fix.

  1. Stop arguing from human count; do the agent-population coordination analysis. State explicitly how many acting agents, how many cross-Domain interaction surfaces, and what governs them.
  2. Name a thin but real integration function in the Cortex — an explicit human-held (for now) coordinator/chief-of-staff role whose job is cross-Domain conflict resolution and priority routing (the "hard 20%" of Finding 1). This is not rebuilding the pyramid; it is admitting the irreducible minimum chain. The framework even already lists chief-of-staff and agent-orchestrator agents — the model demotes agent-orchestrator to "a platform capability" (Domain 9) and dissolves coordination into memory, removing the one role built to do this.
  3. Add conflict-detection to Domain 2: surface when two Domains have acted on contradictory premises, rather than silently storing both.

7. Fidelity gaps: where the model contradicts the framework or the founders

The model is mostly faithful, but several claims overreach or quietly contradict the source.

  • "Consulting needs no org function" overshoots the framework. The model (§1) says consulting "does not need its own org function" and folds it entirely into venture-building/Network. But the framework (§3.2 and Q7) explicitly preserves standalone advisory as an exception ("where the impact could be significant for society and the work does not lend itself to being a venture"). By absorbing consulting wholesale, the model has no home for the founder-sanctioned exception. Minor, but a fidelity slip. Fix: keep an explicit (rare) standalone-advisory path under Domain 8.
  • "Radiates, does not descend" is rhetorical relabeling, not a structural difference. The model leans on the framework's "worldview at the centre, radiates not descends" diagram to claim the Cortex is "not above" the Domains. But the Cortex sets intent, bounds authority, judges escalations, and can halt — those are the powers of a superior, whatever the geometry of the diagram. Calling top-down authority "radiating from the centre" does not change what it is. The framework's "radiates" claim is about conceptual dependency (operations derive from worldview), not about authority direction; the model imports a worldview-diagram metaphor to soften an authority relationship it actually retains. This matters because pretending authority is "central not above" obscures accountability — the opposite of the legible authority the model elsewhere (correctly) demands. Major (it muddies the very accountability the model is built to protect). Fix: drop the "not above" framing for authority; say plainly that the Cortex holds bounded authority over Domains, and reserve "radiates" for conceptual derivation only.
  • Small-team commitment. Founders (Q13) commit that FTLAB itself stays a small human team to a large agentic workforce, with venture teams outside FTLAB headcount. The two-tier model (§6) is faithful here — good. But the ten-Domain studio structure (Finding 4) strains the "small human team" commitment by requiring more human governance bandwidth than a small team has. Already covered in Finding 4.
  • Attention constraint. The framework names attention as the binding constraint and Cognitive Overhead as the core KPI. The model invokes both, then designs a ten-Domain/thirty-role governance load that maximises cognitive overhead. This is the sharpest framework-contradiction in the document. Covered in Findings 4 and 5; flagged here as a fidelity failure, not just a sizing issue.
  • Governance-at-scale open question. The framework (§5.3) lists "how does governance scale across a portfolio without hierarchical bureaucracy" as an open question. The model presents its federation answer ("the pattern repeats, the chain does not lengthen") with more confidence than "open question" warrants, and without testing it. Fix: mark the federation claim as a hypothesis to be tested on venture #2–3, consistent with the framework's own posture of conviction-without-rigidity.

8. Failure modes and second-order effects the model does not address

  1. Memory poisoning / compounding error. An incorrect "validated finding" admitted to Domain 2 propagates to every Domain that reads it; with agents acting on it autonomously, a single bad write becomes institution-wide action before a human notices. No rollback, no quarantine, no blast-radius limit is specified.
  2. Charter drift via precedent. Charters widen "on evidence of verified performance." But "verified by whom, against what bar?" If Domains (or their agents) accumulate small autonomous wins, the envelope widens by ratchet, and no single widening looks like crossing a line — yet the cumulative effect crosses many. The "never" line is protected; the escalate→autonomous boundary is not.
  3. Inter-venture conflict (federation's blind spot). The model federates the pattern to ventures but gives no mechanism for conflict between two ventures (or between a venture and the studio) — e.g., two portfolio ventures competing for the same sovereign relationship, or contradicting each other publicly. Federation copies the unit; it does not coordinate the copies.
  4. Legitimacy under crisis. The framework's own open question asks how the small-team model performs "under crisis conditions … beyond training distributions." A memory-coordinated, agent-run org is least able to coordinate exactly when reality goes off-distribution and the memory's precedents stop applying — the moment a chain of command is most valuable. The model devolves most under calm and has no "command mode" for crisis.
  5. Founder key-person risk. With the Guardian, the integration function, and most Triumvirate roles concentrated in 2–5 people (realistically, largely Shayan), the model has a severe single-point-of-failure that the apprenticeship Domain is meant to address but cannot yet staff. The model identifies the need and has no near-term answer.
  6. External legibility paradox. §7 recommends "Managing/General Partner internally; CEO-equivalent for external legibility." But sovereign partners and regulators (the audience the legitimacy-floor argument is built for) will ask "who is accountable?" A model that is internally "no one commands, the memory coordinates" but externally presents a CEO creates a gap between the real governance and the represented governance — itself an incoherence the framework forbids (§1.5). Fix: make the external story true — name the accountable human(s) and don't dress federation as a conventional CEO line.

What survives the critique

Several elements are genuinely robust and should be kept:

  • "Legitimacy, not capability" as the basis of the permanent floor. This is the strongest idea in the document. It correctly distinguishes what can be devolved (doing, coordinating) from what cannot (legal/relational answerability, values authorship), and it answers the framework's own open question better than the framework does. Keep it — just protect it from erosion (Finding 5).
  • Deriving the org from the work, not from the book. The method (work → functions → roles → governance → evolution) is the right order, and the explicit demotion of the AI-Born book from specification to direction is exactly correct and well-marked.
  • Charters as widening envelopes with a fixed "never" line. The mechanism for devolution-as-Charter-widening is a clean, auditable construct. The "never" line being immovable is right. (The escalate→autonomous boundary needs the protection it lacks — Finding 8.2.)
  • Build the institutional memory early (content) and treat it as foundational. The instinct that coordination depends on a real, current knowledge substrate is correct — the model just under-specifies what that takes (Finding 2) and mis-times when to devolve it (Finding 5).
  • Two-tier federation of the same unit. Reusing one Domain/Triumvirate/Charter pattern for studio and ventures is elegant and faithful to founder intent (venture teams outside FTLAB headcount). It needs an inter-venture conflict mechanism (Finding 8.3), but the pattern is sound.
  • Naming the four genuine gaps (builder/operator, capital/finance, platform engineering, apprenticeship). These are real and correctly identified as what an institution that builds and operates must have.
  • Surfacing the corpus contradictions and the Guardian-independence decision as founder calls. Intellectually honest; consistent with conviction-without-rigidity.

Verdict

Salvageable with significant changes — sound in posture and in two or three core ideas, but flawed at its load-bearing mechanism and over-scaled for its team.

The model is not flawed at the foundation in the sense of starting from a wrong premise — its method is right, its honesty is real, and its "legitimacy floor" is a genuine contribution. But its central construction claim (ten autonomous Domains cohere via shared memory instead of a chain) rests on two things that do not currently hold: a coordination substrate that is asserted rather than engineered (Finding 2), and a human-count argument that hides the agent-population coordination problem it must actually solve (Finding 6). Combined with an arithmetically impossible Guardian independence at 2–5 people (Finding 3) and a governance load that violates the framework's own scarcest-resource principle (Finding 4), the model as written would, in practice, drift into an illegible flat collective of agents — the exact failure it set out to avoid.

It is salvageable because the fixes are concrete and do not require abandoning the thesis.

The 3–5 changes that would most strengthen it:

  1. Engineer the keystone, then prove it small before betting big. Specify Domain 2 as a coordination system (write-discipline, freshness SLA, conflict-resolution protocol with a named human adjudicator, provenance model) and use it first to resolve the institution's own existing contradictions (ratio; Five Planes; CLAUDE.md vs framework). If it cannot hold three definitions straight, it cannot coordinate ten Domains. Devolve it last, not first.
  2. Add the irreducible minimum chain. Name a thin, human-held integration/coordination role in the Cortex that owns cross-Domain conflict resolution and attention-routing (the "hard 20%"). Reinstate agent-orchestrator/chief-of-staff as that coordinator rather than dissolving coordination into memory. This is not a pyramid; it is honesty about what cannot be automated yet.
  3. Right-size the Domains to the team. Collapse to ~5–6 Domains now; publish an attention budget naming the human holding each Triumvirate role today; forbid "nobody" as an owner. Let Domains split as humans are added.
  4. Make Guardian independence real or admit it isn't yet. Externalise the Guardian (board member / external reviewer / sovereign-acceptable third party with an exercisable halt right), or define a binding conflict-of-interest protocol for the founder-as-Guardian interim — and log the gap as a known risk, not a solved one.
  5. Protect the floor from silent erosion. Define a floor-integrity test (can the accountable human reconstruct and defend what they sign?), tie rising Cognitive Overhead / Alignment Debt to a mandatory devolution slowdown, and require Values/"never" lines to be human-originated and human-changeable only.

Held in the framework's own spirit: this model is worth testing in action — after the keystone is engineered and the team-size arithmetic is faced honestly.
