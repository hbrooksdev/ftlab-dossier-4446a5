Option 3 · the integration

The Integrated Architecture

Option 1’s legible face on Option 2’s engineered Core — reached by a phased sequence, with four upgrades, aimed at the Option 4 destination.

Option 311 min readClassification · Strategic / InternalSource · ftlab-org-architecture-option-3-integrated-v0.1.md
In brief

The recognition that Options 1 and 2 are one design at two altitudes: Option 1’s named Domains ≈ Option 2’s Engines; its Cortex ≈ the Edge; its D2 ≈ the Core, minus the engineering. So keep the legible named Domains + Cortex as the human-facing accountability face, run the engineered Core as the coordination engine underneath, reach it by a phased sequence, and wire in the four “steal-able” upgrades.

Key takeaways
  • Deploy Option 1 now; build its keystone (D2) to Option 2’s Core spec from day zero — both rollouts’ first step is the same build.
  • Closes Option 1’s single Critical pressure-test finding (“memory-as-coordinator asserted, not engineered”).
  • Keep both faces permanently: named seats for legal/regulator legibility; the Core for O(reads) coordination — one structure, two labels, one mapping table.
  • Wire in: priced human attention, reputation-as-autonomy, Values-as-code, a pressure/selection signal.

This is the integrated option, documented as a standalone design so it can be reviewed on its own. It is not a third unrelated architecture — it is the recognition that Option 1 (Seven Domains) and Option 2 (Core / Engines / Edge) are one design seen at two altitudes, and the specification of how to run them as a single system: Option 1's named, legible Domains and human Cortex as the human-facing accountability face, running on Option 2's engineered Core as the coordination engine underneath — reached by a phased sequence, with four upgrades wired in, and aimed at the Option 4 destination. Read Options 1, 2, and 4 first; this integrates them.

1. The core idea

Option 1 answers "who is accountable for what?" Option 2 answers "how does coordination physically happen?" An organisation needs both answers, and — critically — they are the same structure described at two altitudes, so combining them is not a compromise but a completion. The mapping is near-exact:

The load-bearing insight: Option 1 lacks an explicit Core. It puts the coordination substrate inside one Domain (D2), as "the integrator + institutional memory." Its own pressure-test rated that the single Critical flaw — "memory-as-coordinator is asserted, not engineered." Option 2's Core is exactly that missing engineering, lifted out of the department layer and built as a real substrate (freshness, role/confidence conflict-merge, provenance, out-of-band verification, a boundary layer). So the integration is one move: take Option 1's accountability layer (Domains + Cortex) and re-seat it on Option 2's properly-engineered Core, instead of on the hand-wavy coordination buried in D2.

2. The end-state: the permanent hybrid

This is the structure FTLAB runs on once integrated — worth stating on its own, because the answer to "do the named Domains disappear once the Core exists?" is no, keep them, permanently. Legibility and coordination-efficiency are different requirements with different audiences, satisfied by two artifacts of one structure:

  • Outward / upward (regulators, sovereigns, board, new hires, the founders' own legal accountability): present the named Domains + human Cortex. "Who owns research? D1. Who can halt on a values breach? The human Guardian. Who is the natural person liable for this sovereign commitment? Named, in the Cortex." A director must be a natural person; GDPR Art. 22 voids rubber-stamps — these are answered by named seats, not abstract functions. The Domains are the accountability ontology: a fixed, legible, externally-defensible map of who answers for what.
  • Inward / operationally (how work actually moves): everything runs through the Core. No Domain routes information to another Domain; they read/write shared state (O(reads)). Topology is task-matched. Verification is a first-class Core layer. The Domains are labels on regions of the Core's capability graph, not boxes in a reporting chain.

The one permanent structural change: D2 is demoted from "the Domain that coordinates" to "the steward of the Core." Coordination stops being something a Domain does and becomes something the substrate is. This dissolves Option 1's two Critical findings at once — coordination is now engineered (the Core), and is no longer a single-point-of-failure Domain devolved early. The Domains keep their names and their accountability; they lose only the pretence that one of them is the coordination mechanism.

Why keep both faces forever rather than collapse to pure Option 2: the named Domains cost almost nothing to retain (labels + charters + the Edge governance question applied per region) and buy three permanent goods pure-Option-2 lacks — external legibility / legal defensibility, a stable accountability ontology that doesn't shift each time the Core's module boundaries are refined, and a human-comprehensible onboarding/governance surface. The named layer is the standing antidote to the coordination architecture becoming illegible.

3. The path: the phased sequence

The hybrid is the destination; this is how you get there from where FTLAB is today (Option 1 deployable; Option 2's Core a build). The two rollout plans already interlock — Option 1's Step 1 is "build D2's memory substrate first"; Option 2's Phase 0 is "build C1 artifact substrate → C2 world model first." These are the same first move. The sequence simply declares that when you build D2 first, you build it to the Core spec.

Phase 0 — Foundations. Deploy the legible org; build the substrate to spec.

  • Stand up Option 1's seven Domains from the staged definitions (28→7 elevation, model-tiering, the verification-gated rollback — keep the 28 until the 7 verify). FTLAB has a legible, named, accountable, value-producing org in weeks.
  • But build D2 to Option 2's Core specification, not Option 1's: C1 artifact substrate (day zero) → C2a boundary layer (the per-decision-class "model informs vs. human decides" table, written before the memory is trusted) → C2 world-model-as-a-database (freshness, role/confidence conflict-merge, provenance, out-of-band verification) → C3 capability registry. This closes the Critical finding on day one.
  • D2's first job (unchanged): run the canonical-terms resolution (the ratio stated three ways; the two "Five Planes" definitions; the phantom agents). Cheap proof that the substrate can hold the institution's own terms straight before it coordinates anything.
  • Deploy the integrator as the Core's C4 routing + C5 verification pair, chartered exactly as Option 1 already chartered it (route and surface; never resolve values; escalate contested routing).
  • Exit trigger: the Core passes Option 2's verification checklist on the studio itself. Until then, D2 stays human-curated and the org runs on Option 1's charters as written.

Phase 1 — Coordination proven. Begin lifting coordination out of D2-the-Domain into the Core-the-substrate: the other Domains stop querying D2 as a department and start reading/writing shared state (O(reads)). Adopt Option 2's interaction model (protocol/SOP structured-artifact hand-offs; topology-by-task — parallelise reads, single-thread writes; risk-tiered escalation with the hard guardrails — no external email / prod-push / binding external commitment without a human). Move the Cortex toward separation-of-powers (Direction / Verification / Governance held by different humans), and set a dated externalisation trigger for the Guardian halt-right. Trigger: cross-Domain coordination through shared state matches or beats the human-curated path on a measured task class; Cognitive Overhead flat or falling.

Phase 2 — Migrate Domains → Engines, on one venture first. Re-cut the Domains into Engines by capability/module boundary (the mirroring hypothesis): D1→E1; D3→E2; D6 splits into E3+E5; surface E4; D4 becomes the builder of the Core; D5 splits into C5 + the Edge's human functions; D7's people/apprenticeship → Edge, finance → Core-instrumented. Do it on one venture first (instrument decision quality, pre-commit a rollback criterion), then template. Wire the deal engine: composition-failure → (capability-roadmap item + candidate venture thesis). Trigger: one venture validated through a full cycle on a Core/Engines/Edge instance.

Phase 3 — Fractal scale-out + measured devolution. Instantiate Core/Engines/Edge per new venture; coordinate the portfolio through the thin meta-model + protocols, not a chain. Widen the autonomous tier by measured reliability per task class (research/coordination first; synthesis/build; external/irreversible last). The Edge thins to the irreducible legitimacy-and-purpose minimum; apprenticeship is the one Edge function that grows.

4. The four upgrades, wired in (from Option 4)

The integration is the moment to adopt Option 4's four "steal-ables" — each improves the hybrid at low cost and bends it toward the destination:

  1. Price human attention. Give every Domain/Engine an attention budget it must spend to escalate to a human. Converts the lagging "Cognitive Overhead" metric into a live allocation mechanism — the framework's scarcest resource, priced.
  2. Reputation as a first-class object. Replace static charters/risk-tiers with a per-agent, per-region reputation + validation registry. Devolution stops being a quarterly founder decision and becomes continuous, earned, and reversible — autonomy rises with verified track record and falls on failure.
  3. Values compiled to rules-as-code at the membrane. Make the non-negotiable Values machine-enforced at the protocol boundary, binding regardless of model/vendor — Values-Conscious Architecture as an enforced invariant, not prose.
  4. A pressure/selection signal on the substrate. Let the world model drive coordination for decomposable task classes (pressure = measured badness), not just inform it — importing the stigmergy result without adopting a full internal market.

These also make the eventual move toward Option 4 (the market/genome destination) a continuation rather than a rebuild.

5. What it reuses (so it is deployable, not a fresh build)

Option 3 is deployable now because it orchestrates existing artifacts rather than building new ones:

  • From Option 1: the seven staged Head definitions (rollout/agents/), the 28→7 elevation map, the canonical-terms resolution, the venture template, the verification-gated rollback. These are the accountability face.
  • From Option 2: the Core build-spec (C1–C5), the Core-first rollout, the five staged Engine definitions + the routing and verification agents (rollout-option2/agents/), the interaction protocols. These are the coordination engine.
  • New work is small and integrative: one canonical mapping table (Domains↔Engines, Cortex↔Edge, D2↔Core) maintained as the single source of truth; the four upgrades; and the phase triggers.

6. Assumptions, likely functioning, evolution

Assumptions: the Domain↔Engine and Cortex↔Edge mappings are as clean in practice as on paper (high confidence — explicit in both docs); the Core can be engineered to spec (medium, explicitly gated — de-risked by proving it on the canonical-terms job first); running a legible face over an efficient substrate does not create a gap between represented and real governance (medium-high if the mapping table is the single source of truth); founders hold separation-of-powers at 2–5 people or externalise as they grow (medium — a logged, known risk).

Likely functioning: seven named, accountable Domains under a small human Cortex (the org chart anyone can read), all of whose coordination happens by reading/writing a shared engineered world model with verification before every autonomous boundary. Founders govern by policy and exception. New work routes by topology-matched composition; coordination cost scales O(reads); gaps become venture theses; each venture is the same thing in miniature.

Evolution: the autonomous tier widens by measured reliability (research/coordination first, external/irreversible last). The named Domains stay fixed (the accountability ontology) while the Core's internals and Engine boundaries evolve underneath — exactly the right thing to hold stable (legibility) vs. let move (implementation). End-state: a near-fully-autonomous Core-and-Engines under the smallest legitimate human Edge/Cortex, apprenticeship the one function that grew — with the four upgrades already bending it toward the Option 4 market/genome form.

7. Risks of integrating, and mitigations

RiskMitigation
Two vocabularies → confusion ("Domains or Engines?")One canonical mapping table as the single source of truth; rule: Domains/Cortex = accountability names; Core/Engines/Edge = implementation. Every charter row points at its boundary-layer row.
Half-built Core is worse than none (silent year-two failure)Hard gate: until the Core passes verification, D2 stays human-curated and the org runs on Option 1's charters; the boundary layer is written before the model coordinates; promote each task-class only on measured reliability.
Migration cost / double-runningMost of it is one build, not two (Option 1's "build D2 first" = Option 2's "build C1/C2 first"); keep the reversible rollback; migrate one venture first.
The legible face becomes a lie (real power elsewhere)Face and substrate are the same structure (the mapping table); keep separation-of-powers so authority is named; audit represented-vs-actual governance as a standing Edge function.
Guardian independence unsolved at 2–5 peopleFounders hold it jointly under a logged COI protocol now; dated externalisation trigger; track as Alignment Debt. (Inherited from both options; not created by integrating.)

The integration adds no new fatal risk; it removes Option 1's Critical keystone risk and Option 2's readiness/legibility risk.

8. Build steps and founder decisions

Build steps (turnkey): follow Option 1's rollout to stand up the seven Domains, substituting Option 2's Core build (Phase 0 above) for Option 1's "build D2's memory" step; then Phases 1–3 as in §3; wire the four upgrades at Phase 1; maintain the canonical mapping table from day one.

Founder decisions:

  1. Commit to building D2 as the engineered Core from day zero? (the central bet of Option 3)
  2. Adopt the four upgrades now? (priced attention + reputation-as-autonomy are design-shaping)
  3. Guardian separation-of-powers + a dated externalisation trigger?
  4. Confirm the permanent hybrid as the end-state (keep the named Domains forever as the accountability face), vs. eventually collapsing to pure Option 2.
  5. How explicitly to aim at the Option 4 destination.

9. Why this is the recommendation

Option 1 ships a Critical unbuilt keystone; Option 2 ships an abstract, not-yet-proven architecture with a long pre-value build; Option 3 ships Option 1's legible, reversible, value-producing org now while building Option 2's engineered Core underneath, closing the one Critical finding and paying neither's full penalty. It captures Option 1's readiness and legibility and Option 2's coordination rigour, lands on the end-state both documents already concede is compatible, and — with the four upgrades — is already walking toward the Option 4 destination. In one line: ship the Domains, build the Core under them, wire in priced-attention + reputation + rules-as-code, converge to the hybrid, steer toward the market/genome — proving each step on one venture first.
